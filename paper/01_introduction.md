# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 The Puzzle of Parasitic Legitimacy

On October 15, 2025, I received an invitation to submit my paper "Law as Extended Phenotype: Reframing Legal Theory Through Evolutionary Biology" to the *Journal of Computational Law and Legal Technology* (JCLLT), an emerging open-access journal published by Bon View Publishing. The invitation was professional, correctly cited my SSRN publication, and aligned precisely with my research area. After verification, I accepted the invitation.

Ten days later, I received another invitation to submit the same paper to the *International Journal of Financial and Economic Studies* (IJFES), published by Academix Publishers. This invitation cited the identical SSRN paper but proposed publishing it in an **economics** journal. The email contained template placeholders ("Dear Dr. <Name>"), arrived from a Gmail address, and proposed a discipline with zero connection to my manuscript. I declined immediately.

Over the following six weeks, I received seven more invitations. Six followed the IJFES pattern: dramatic disciplinary mismatches (statistics, journalism, geosciences), mimetic legitimacy signals (ISSN numbers, claims of rapid peer review), and automated scraping indicators (exact title reproduction with no contextual understanding). One invitation explicitly disclosed an article processing charge of USD 198 without specifying peer review standards. The seventh replicated the JCLLT pattern: legitimate alignment, transparent policies, and verifiable infrastructure.

This corpus presents a theoretical puzzle. Standard explanations of academic fraud, principal-agent problems, or information asymmetry do not account for three critical features:

1. **Stability despite exposure:** Predatory publishing persists and grows despite widespread public exposure, blacklisting efforts (Beall's List), and institutional warnings. If this were simple fraud or information failure, transparency would reduce prevalence. Instead, the opposite occurs.

2. **Functional mimicry without function:** Predatory journals replicate the **form** of legitimate publishing (ISSN, DOI, peer review claims) while entirely abandoning the **epistemic function** (quality validation). Why maintain costly mimicry if the function is abandoned?

3. **Content irrelevance:** The Diago case study (Appendix A) demonstrates that predatory journals accept absurd, nonsensical manuscripts for publication. If the business model were to publish low-quality but legitimate research, content would matter. It does not.

This paper proposes that predatory publishing is not anomalous fraud but an **evolutionarily stable strategy** within credential-dependent systems. Specifically, predatory journals exemplify **Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO)**: an emergent institutional configuration that evolves through selection pressures inherent in metric-driven academic ecosystems, replicating the phenotypic signals of legitimate institutions while extracting resources without providing corresponding epistemic services.

## 1.2 Extended Phenotype Theory: Core Mechanism

Richard Dawkins' *The Extended Phenotype* (1982) argues that natural selection operates not on organisms but on **replicators** (genes), and that phenotypic effects extend beyond the organism's body to include environmental structures that enhance replicator survival. The classic example: beaver dams are not merely "tools" built by beavers but **extended phenotypes** of beaver genes, selected because they increase gene replication by creating favorable environments.

Three core principles structure Extended Phenotype Theory (EPT):

**Principle 1: Gene's-Eye View**  
Selection acts on replicators (genes), not vehicles (organisms). The relevant question is not "What helps the organism?" but "What helps the gene replicate?"

**Principle 2: Phenotypic Extension**  
Gene effects extend beyond the organism to include manipulations of the environment, other organisms, or institutional structures. A parasite that alters host behavior expresses the parasite's genes through the host's phenotype.

**Principle 3: Functional Alignment vs. Divergence**  
Extended phenotypes may align with host interests (mutualism) or diverge (parasitism). Cuckoo chicks manipulating host birds to provision them exemplify divergence: the host's behavior serves the cuckoo's genes, not the host's.

EPT has been successfully applied to social institutions through **memetics** (Dawkins 1976; Blackmore 1999): cultural replicators ("memes") compete for transmission, and institutions function as extended phenotypes of successful memes. Legal systems, monetary regimes, and academic structures can be analyzed as extended phenotypes of underlying incentive structures (Lerer 2024a).

This paper applies EPT to predatory publishing mechanistically, not metaphorically. The replicator is the **"published paper" token**—a cultural unit that signals academic productivity and replicates through institutional recognition. The extended phenotype is the **journal structure** that processes and validates these tokens. In legitimate systems, the phenotype aligns with epistemic function (quality validation). In predatory systems, the phenotype diverges: it mimics legitimacy signals to extract resources (author fees, credential inflation) while abandoning validation function.

## 1.3 Spontaneous Order: Institutional Emergence Without Design

F.A. Hayek's concept of **spontaneous order** (1973) describes institutional configurations that emerge through decentralized individual actions without central coordination. Markets, languages, and common law exemplify spontaneous orders: no single actor designs them, yet they exhibit structure, stability, and functionality.

Hayek distinguished **two types of spontaneous order:**

1. **Beneficial spontaneous order (BSO):** Emerges from adaptive pressures that align individual incentives with collective welfare. Example: market prices aggregate dispersed information, improving resource allocation.

2. **Parasitic spontaneous order (PSO):** Emerges from adaptive pressures that exploit institutional vulnerabilities without contributing proportional value. Example: regulatory capture, where interest groups exploit rule-making processes to extract rents.

The critical distinction: **PSO arises not from conspiracy but from selection.** No mastermind coordinates predatory publishing; instead, journals that successfully mimic legitimacy signals while minimizing costly validation processes outcompete both (a) journals that invest in genuine review and (b) journals that signal illegitimacy and fail to attract submissions. PSO is an **evolutionarily stable strategy** within dysfunctional incentive environments.

## 1.4 Convergence: EPT + Spontaneous Order = Institutional Analysis

The convergence of EPT and spontaneous order theory offers a general framework for analyzing institutional parasitism (Lerer 2024b). Key synthesis:

| **Component** | **EPT Mechanism** | **Spontaneous Order Mechanism** | **Synthesis** |
|---------------|-------------------|--------------------------------|---------------|
| **Replicator** | Gene / Meme | Institutional practice | Published paper token |
| **Phenotype** | Extended structure | Emergent rule system | Journal infrastructure |
| **Selection** | Differential replication | Competitive advantage | Author choice + institutional reward |
| **Parasitism** | Functional divergence | Resource extraction | Credential vending without validation |
| **Stability** | Evolutionarily stable | Nash equilibrium | Persistent despite exposure |

This framework predicts three empirical patterns:

**Prediction 1: Mimetic fidelity increases with transparency**  
As blacklists and exposure efforts improve, PSO entities invest more in mimicry (better ISSN acquisition, professional website design, fabricated editorial boards) to evade detection. This generates an arms race, not elimination.

**Prediction 2: Content quality is irrelevant**  
Because PSO journals extract value from credential vending, not epistemic contribution, manuscript content should not predict acceptance. The Diago experiment (Appendix A) validates this: absurd papers are accepted as readily as legitimate ones.

**Prediction 3: Disciplinary mismatch is stable**  
Because PSO journals scrape publications automatically and target authors indiscriminately, dramatic field mismatches (law paper to geoscience journal) should persist despite zero acceptance probability from discerning authors. PSO optimizes for volume, not precision.

## 1.5 Methodological Approach: Corpus Analysis + Diagnostic Tool Development

This paper analyzes **eight invitation emails** received over six weeks (October–December 2025) by the author. The corpus is not representative but is **information-rich:** it includes one legitimate control case (JCLLT) and seven cases exhibiting classic predatory patterns, allowing systematic comparison.

The methodological strategy combines:

1. **Qualitative pattern identification:** Six categories of parasitic signals are documented (disciplinary mismatch, mimetic legitimacy, emotional hooks, friction minimization, automation, financial extraction).

2. **Quantitative diagnostic tool:** The **Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO) Index** assigns binary scores (0/1) for each of five patterns (excluding financial extraction, which is not always disclosed), producing a 0–5 scale. Cases scoring 4–5 are classified as PSO.

3. **External validation:** The Diago case study (Appendix A) provides independent confirmation: a researcher submitted an intentionally absurd paper to a high-PSO journal, which accepted it without substantive review, confirming functional divergence.

4. **Stress testing:** Ten theoretical objections (Appendix B) are addressed to strengthen the framework's robustness.

## 1.6 Contribution and Significance

This paper makes **four core contributions:**

**1. Theoretical reframing**  
Predatory publishing is reconceptualized from isolated fraud to **systemic parasitism**, grounded in evolutionary mechanisms (EPT) and institutional economics (spontaneous order). This reframing explains persistence, diversification, and content-blindness.

**2. Diagnostic tool**  
The PSO Index provides practitioners (researchers, librarians, administrators) with a quantitative instrument for real-time classification. In this corpus, it achieves **100% accuracy** (8/8 cases correctly classified).

**3. Policy implications**  
Standard approaches (blacklists, author education) treat symptoms. The EPT framework identifies root causes: metric-driven incentives that reward publication volume over quality. Effective solutions must modify selection environments (institutional hiring/promotion criteria, funding allocation rules) rather than react to individual PSO entities.

**4. Generalizability**  
The PSO framework extends beyond academic publishing to other domains exhibiting parasitic dynamics: citation rings (artificial citation inflation), conference mills (credential vending at pay-to-present events), ranking manipulation (journal impact factor gaming), and diploma mills (degree credentialing without education). Section 6 outlines these extensions.

## 1.8 Reality Filter: Limitations and Scope Conditions

This study is **not** a representative sample of predatory invitations. The corpus reflects:

1. **Author characteristics:** Senior legal scholar, visible SSRN presence, specialized interdisciplinary work (law + evolutionary biology).

2. **Temporal window:** October–December 2025, coinciding with increased visibility of SSRN papers.

3. **Language and region:** English-language invitations, likely scraped from global databases.

The PSO Index is validated within this corpus (100% accuracy) but requires **independent replication** across:

- Different disciplines (STEM, humanities, social sciences beyond law/economics)
- Different career stages (early-career researchers, graduate students, non-Western scholars)
- Different publication platforms (preprint servers beyond SSRN, institutional repositories)

The framework's **theoretical claims** (stability, mimicry, content-blindness) are grounded in evolutionary logic and validated by the Diago case, but **quantitative prevalence** (what percentage of all invitations are PSO?) is not estimated here. That requires large-scale data collection beyond this paper's scope.

Finally, this paper does **not** claim that all open-access publishing is predatory, that all rapid peer review is illegitimate, or that all new journals are suspect. Case #1 (JCLLT) demonstrates that emerging journals can exhibit full transparency and epistemic integrity. The PSO framework identifies **functional divergence**, not business model or newness.
