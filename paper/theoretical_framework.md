# Theoretical Framework

## The Extended Phenotype Concept: From Biology to Institutions

### Origins in Evolutionary Biology

The concept of the extended phenotype emerged from a fundamental insight in evolutionary theory. Richard Dawkins (1982) observed that genes produce effects not only within the bodies that house them but also in the external world. These extended effects, when they enhance gene replication, are subject to natural selection just as much as traditional phenotypic traits. The classic examples include:

- **Beaver dams**: A beaver's genes influence dam-building behavior, which modifies the environment to enhance survival and reproduction of those same genes.
- **Caddis fly larval cases**: Genes in the caddis fly determine the construction of protective cases from environmental materials, extending the phenotype beyond the organism's body.
- **Parasitic manipulation**: Genes in parasitic organisms manipulate host behavior to enhance parasite transmission. The classic case is *Toxoplasma gondii*, which alters rodent behavior to reduce fear of cats, facilitating transmission to the definitive host.

The theoretical revolution lies in recognizing that *selection operates on effects, not merely on physical structures*. A gene that causes a beaver to build a better dam is selected for the dam's downstream consequences (flood control, predator protection, food storage), even though the dam exists outside the beaver's body. The unit of selection is not the organism but the *replicator* (the gene), and the vehicle of selection can be any phenotypic effect that influences replicator fitness.

This insight has profound implications when generalized beyond biology. Any system subject to evolutionary dynamics (variation, selection, inheritance) will produce extended phenotypes if downstream effects systematically influence replication. The question is not whether extended phenotypes exist in cultural or institutional domains, but rather how to identify them and predict their effects.

### Application to Institutional Systems

Lerer (2024a, 2024b) develops the formal application of Extended Phenotype Theory to legal and institutional systems. The key theoretical moves are:

1. **Institutional replicators**: Legal rules, governance structures, and organizational forms replicate through imitation, enforcement, and path dependency. Just as genes replicate through DNA copying, institutions replicate through cultural transmission, precedent, and isomorphic pressures (DiMaggio & Powell, 1983).

2. **Selection pressures**: Institutional forms face selection based on their effects, not their intentions or explicit goals. Rules that increase their own enforcement, precedents that expand their own applicability, and organizational forms that attract resources will out-replicate alternatives, regardless of social welfare effects.

3. **Extended phenotypic effects**: Institutions produce downstream consequences (compliance behavior, resource flows, coordination patterns) that feed back to influence institutional replication. These effects constitute the institutional extended phenotype.

4. **Differential fitness**: Just as biological fitness is measured by reproductive success (copies of genes in the next generation), institutional fitness is measured by replicative success (adoption rates, persistence, resistance to reform). An institution that harms its participants but effectively replicates will out-compete welfare-enhancing alternatives that replicate poorly.

The critical insight is that *institutional evolution need not track social welfare*. Biological evolution produces maladaptive traits (peacock tails that hinder flight but attract mates; evolutionary arms races that consume resources without net benefit). Institutional evolution similarly produces pathological forms that persist precisely because their extended phenotypic effects enhance their own replication while imposing net costs on participants.

### Game-Theoretic Foundations

The evolutionary stability of parasitic institutional forms can be formalized using evolutionary game theory. Consider a population of researchers choosing between three publication strategies:

- **Elite Strategy (E)**: Submit only to top-tier journals with rigorous peer review (high rejection rates, long timelines, high prestige).
- **Mainstream Strategy (M)**: Submit to mid-tier legitimate journals with standard review (moderate acceptance, moderate timelines, moderate prestige).
- **Parasitic Strategy (P)**: Submit to predatory journals with guaranteed acceptance (immediate publication, minimal review, negligible prestige).

Payoffs depend on institutional context. Under a publication-count metric regime (common in tenure reviews, visa requirements, and many developing-country institutions), strategies yield:

```
Payoff(E) = α * 0.2 (high prestige) - β * 24 (months to publication) + γ * 1 (career boost if successful)
Payoff(M) = α * 0.5 (moderate prestige) - β * 8 (months) + γ * 0.5 (moderate career boost)
Payoff(P) = α * 0.0 (zero prestige) - β * 1 (immediate publication) + γ * 1 (publication count)
```

Where α measures reputational weight, β measures time-discount, and γ measures count-based rewards.

In environments where γ dominates (publication counts matter more than prestige) and β is high (time pressure is severe), the parasitic strategy becomes evolutionarily stable. Researchers with tenure-track pressure, visa renewal deadlines, or institutional publication requirements face payoff structures where P-strategy dominates.

Crucially, **this is not a Prisoner's Dilemma**. In a Prisoner's Dilemma, mutual cooperation yields higher payoffs than mutual defection, but individual incentives favor defection. Here, the parasitic equilibrium emerges because *institutional rules create payoff structures that genuinely reward parasitic strategies*. Researchers who submit to predatory journals may be acting optimally given their constraints. The pathology lies in the institutional design, not individual rationality.

This analysis reveals why fraud-detection frameworks fail. If researchers knowingly submit to suspect venues because institutional incentives reward publication counts over quality, then educating researchers about predatory journals will not change behavior. The solution requires changing the payoff structure through metric reform, not merely increasing awareness.

## Parasitic Spontaneous Order: Conceptual Architecture

### Spontaneous Order in Social Theory

The concept of spontaneous order originates with Hayek (1973, 1976, 1979) and the Austrian school of economics. Spontaneous order refers to complex, adaptive coordination patterns that emerge from decentralized interactions without central planning or conscious design. Classic examples include:

- **Market prices**: No central authority sets prices; they emerge from countless individual transactions and convey distributed information about scarcity and preferences (Hayek, 1945).
- **Language**: Grammatical rules, vocabulary, and conventions evolve through usage without deliberate design by any linguistic authority.
- **Common law**: Legal precedents accumulate through case-by-case adjudication, generating complex doctrinal structures without comprehensive codification.
- **Urban form**: City layouts, neighborhoods, and land use patterns emerge from millions of individual decisions about where to live, work, and build.

Hayek emphasized the *epistemological* advantage of spontaneous order: it aggregates dispersed local knowledge that no central planner could access. Market prices reflect information about supply, demand, production costs, and consumer preferences scattered across millions of agents. Any attempt to replace this decentralized coordination with central planning inevitably loses information and reduces efficiency.

The normative implication is generally positive: spontaneous orders are viewed as efficient, adaptive, and welfare-enhancing compared to top-down alternatives. Hayek's work profoundly influenced neo-liberal economic policy and critiques of regulatory intervention.

### The Dark Side of Spontaneous Order

Yet Hayek himself recognized that not all spontaneous orders are beneficial. In *Law, Legislation and Liberty* (Vol. 1, 1973, pp. 20-21), he distinguishes between orders that facilitate mutually beneficial cooperation (catallaxy) and those that merely exhibit regularity without producing welfare gains. The criterion is not spontaneity per se but whether the order enables participants to achieve their ends more effectively.

Recent work in institutional economics, evolutionary game theory, and complexity science has identified numerous cases where spontaneous orders produce harmful equilibria:

- **Poverty traps**: Decentralized decisions can coordinate around low-productivity equilibria where everyone expects continued poverty, discouraging investment (Bowles, Durlauf & Hoff, 2006).
- **Corruption equilibria**: Where everyone expects corruption, individual incentives favor participating in corrupt exchanges, creating self-reinforcing coordination around rent-seeking (Acemoglu & Robinson, 2012).
- **Arms races**: Spontaneous competitive dynamics can produce equilibria where all parties invest heavily in zero-sum competition, dissipating surplus without net benefit (Schelling, 1960).
- **Veblen effects**: Status-signaling competitions coordinate around conspicuous consumption that absorbs resources without enhancing welfare (Frank, 1999).

These examples share critical features: *coordination without conspiracy* (no one orchestrates the harmful pattern), *individual rationality* (each agent behaves optimally given others' behavior), *collective suboptimality* (all would be better off under a different equilibrium), and *evolutionary stability* (unilateral deviation is punished, so the pattern persists).

### Defining Parasitic Spontaneous Order

Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO) extends this analysis by incorporating Extended Phenotype Theory. A PSO is a spontaneous coordination pattern that:

1. **Emerges without central coordination**: No single actor orchestrates the system; the pattern arises from distributed interactions.

2. **Exhibits self-reinforcing dynamics**: Participation by some agents creates incentives for others to participate, generating positive feedback loops.

3. **Extracts value asymmetrically**: One set of participants systematically transfers resources to another set without receiving commensurate benefits.

4. **Replicates through extended phenotypic effects**: The institutional form produces downstream consequences (information asymmetries, coordination failures, distorted incentives) that enhance the form's own persistence.

5. **Resists elimination**: Because the pattern is evolutionarily stable (no individual has incentive to deviate unilaterally) and structurally adaptive (it evolves countermeasures to interventions), it persists despite widespread recognition of its harmful effects.

6. **Mimics legitimate order**: PSO exploits superficial similarity to welfare-enhancing institutions, making detection costly and imperfect.

The concept is deliberately narrow to avoid over-generalization. Not every harmful spontaneous order is parasitic; some may be tragic dilemmas where mutual gains from cooperation exist but cannot be realized. Not every asymmetric exchange is parasitic; legitimate markets involve value transfers but provide mutual benefits. PSO specifically identifies systems where:

- **One party extracts** (gains resources)
- **Without reciprocal provision** (provides negligible genuine service)
- **Through mimicry** (appears superficially legitimate)
- **Enabled by structural failures** (information asymmetry, coordination failure, distorted metrics)
- **In evolutionarily stable equilibrium** (pattern persists despite awareness)

### Biological Analogy: Brood Parasitism

The biological analogue is instructive. Brood parasites (e.g., cuckoos, cowbirds) lay eggs in other species' nests, exploiting host parental investment while providing no reciprocal benefit. The system persists because:

1. **Mimicry**: Parasite eggs visually resemble host eggs, reducing detection.
2. **Exploitation of existing behavior**: Hosts have evolved strong incubation and feeding instincts; parasites exploit these without triggering counter-adaptations.
3. **Asymmetric costs**: Rejection errors (discarding a host's own egg) are more costly than acceptance errors (raising a parasite), so hosts evolve permissive thresholds.
4. **Evolutionary arms race**: As hosts evolve better discrimination, parasites evolve better mimicry, maintaining equilibrium through continuous adaptation.

Predatory journals operate analogously. They mimic legitimate journals (ISSN numbers, editorial boards, peer-review claims), exploit existing academic behavior (researchers need publications), benefit from asymmetric costs (rejecting a legitimate invitation is more costly than accepting a parasitic one), and engage in evolutionary arms races (as detection improves, mimicry becomes more sophisticated).

The biological analogy illuminates why standard interventions fail. Just as educating birds about cuckoo parasitism would not change host behavior (the underlying evolutionary pressures remain), educating researchers about predatory journals has limited effect when institutional incentives reward publication counts. The solution requires changing the selection pressures, not merely increasing awareness.

## Application to Academic Publishing

### The Institutional Ecology of Academic Publishing

Modern academic publishing operates within a complex institutional ecology shaped by four interlocking systems:

1. **Credentialing and career advancement**: Universities, funding agencies, and professional societies use publication records as primary signals of scholarly productivity. Tenure decisions, grant allocations, and hiring choices depend heavily on publication counts and citation metrics.

2. **Open-access mandates**: Funding agencies and institutions increasingly require open-access publication of research outputs. This creates a market for author-pays models where article processing charges (APCs) replace subscription revenue.

3. **Metric proliferation**: Bibliometric indicators (impact factors, h-indices, citation counts) provide quantitative proxies for research quality. These metrics are embedded in evaluation systems despite well-known limitations and gaming vulnerabilities.

4. **Digital infrastructure**: The Internet dramatically reduces costs of launching new journals, distributing content, and soliciting submissions. Barriers to entry in academic publishing have fallen by orders of magnitude.

These systems create an evolutionary environment with specific selection pressures:

- **Publish-or-perish**: Researchers face strong incentives to maximize publication counts, sometimes at the expense of quality.
- **Revenue pressure**: Publishers seek profitable business models; APCs provide direct revenue per article.
- **Information asymmetry**: Authors have imperfect information about journal quality, editorial rigor, and indexing status.
- **Coordination failure**: No centralized mechanism exists to certify legitimate venues or coordinate rejection of parasitic submissions.

Within this ecology, predatory journals represent a parasitic equilibrium. They exploit every structural vulnerability:

- **Against publish-or-perish pressure**: Predatory journals guarantee rapid publication and acceptance, meeting researcher demands for counts.
- **Against open-access incentives**: They claim to provide open access (technically true) while charging APCs (technically permitted).
- **Against metric gaming**: They inflate citation counts through mutual citation rings, publish fake issues to claim impact factors, and appear in low-quality indexing services.
- **Against information asymmetry**: They mimic legitimate journals' visual presentation, organizational structure, and editorial processes, making detection costly.

### Extended Phenotype of Predatory Publishing

The institutional form "predatory academic journal" has an extended phenotype consisting of:

1. **Solicitation emails**: The invitations studied in this paper are extended phenotypic effects. They are not merely marketing; they are the mechanism by which the institutional form replicates.

2. **Mimetic legitimacy signals**: ISSN numbers, editorial board lists, professional website design, and institutional affiliations function as extended phenotype. These signals exist outside any individual publisher but systematically enhance the form's replication by reducing detection probability.

3. **Acceptance guarantees**: Rapid turnaround times and minimal peer review are extended phenotypic effects that attract submissions. They exploit time-pressure and risk-aversion in author populations.

4. **Citation manipulation**: Through mutual citation rings, predatory journals inflate the citation metrics of participating authors. This extended phenotype enhances replication by providing tangible benefits that offset reputational costs.

5. **Credential inflation**: Predatory publishers offer "editorial board" appointments to researchers, creating CV lines that mimic legitimate service. This extended phenotype recruits participants who benefit from credential padding.

6. **Adaptive mimicry**: As detection mechanisms improve (blacklists, educational campaigns), predatory forms evolve more sophisticated mimicry. They adopt DOI assignment, claim indexing in legitimate databases, and register with CrossRef. This adaptive response is itself an extended phenotype, a downstream effect that enhances replicative fitness.

The key insight is that none of these extended phenotypic effects requires conscious planning by any individual actor. The institutional form evolves because *variations that produce these effects replicate more successfully than variations that do not*. Publishers who send more solicitations get more submissions; journals that mimic legitimacy more closely attract more authors; systems that reduce submission friction increase participation rates. Over time, these features are selected for, regardless of their truth-value or social benefit.

### Why PSO Persists: Evolutionary Stability Analysis

The evolutionary stability of predatory publishing can be analyzed formally. Let:

- **π(L,L)** = payoff to legitimate journals when competing against legitimate journals
- **π(L,P)** = payoff to legitimate journals when competing against predatory journals
- **π(P,L)** = payoff to predatory journals when competing against legitimate journals
- **π(P,P)** = payoff to predatory journals when competing against predatory journals

A parasitic equilibrium exists when:

```
π(P,L) > π(L,L)  AND  π(P,P) > π(L,P)
```

In words: predatory journals out-compete legitimate journals regardless of the opponent's strategy. Why do these inequalities hold?

**Predatory vs. Legitimate (π(P,L) > π(L,L))**:

- **Cost advantage**: Predatory journals incur minimal editorial costs (no substantive peer review, no copyediting, automated processes). Fixed costs are near zero.
- **Revenue per article**: APCs provide immediate revenue; subscription models require long-term customer relationships.
- **Market segmentation**: Predatory journals target researchers who cannot access legitimate venues (early-career scholars, developing-country institutions, time-pressured authors). This is a distinct market niche.
- **Volume**: Predatory journals accept all submissions, maximizing throughput. Legitimate journals reject 70-90% of submissions, limiting volume.

**Predatory vs. Predatory (π(P,P) > π(L,P))**:

- **Continuation**: When predatory journals compete against each other, they engage in mimicry races (better website design, more sophisticated legitimacy signals, more aggressive solicitation). But both continue to extract revenue.
- **Market exhaustion**: As predatory journals saturate the market, marginal profits decline. But legitimate journals face even steeper declines because their cost structures are higher.
- **Switching costs**: Researchers who have already submitted to predatory venues face reputational lock-in; switching to legitimate venues requires explaining prior choices. This creates customer captivity.

The inequalities demonstrate that predatory publishing is an **Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)** in the formal game-theoretic sense (Maynard Smith, 1982). No individual actor can profitably deviate. Researchers cannot unilaterally boycott predatory journals if institutional incentives reward publication counts. Legitimate publishers cannot adopt predatory tactics without destroying their reputational capital. Regulators cannot eliminate predatory journals without addressing the underlying metric distortions that create demand.

This analysis explains why interventions have failed. Blacklists target individual journals but do not change the payoff structure; new journals emerge immediately. Education increases awareness but does not alter incentives; informed researchers still submit when institutional pressures demand publication counts. Whitelisting creates mimicry pressure; predatory journals adopt legitimacy signals to game the certification process.

Effective interventions must change the payoff inequalities. This requires:

1. **Eliminating count-based metrics** (removing the demand side)
2. **Increasing transparency** (reducing information asymmetry)
3. **Coordinating rejections** (changing competitive dynamics)
4. **Structural reform** (altering institutional incentives upstream)

## Cognitive and Structural Mechanisms

### Cognitive Biases Exploited by Parasitic Systems

Parasitic Spontaneous Orders succeed by exploiting systematic patterns in human cognition. Predatory journal invitations specifically target six cognitive biases documented in the behavioral economics and psychology literature:

**1. Authority Bias (Milgram, 1974)**: Humans defer to perceived authority figures. Invitations from "Editorial Board Members" or "Managing Editors" trigger compliance even when the authority is unverified. The mere existence of titles and institutional affiliations activates heuristic acceptance.

**2. Scarcity Bias (Cialdini, 2006)**: Items presented as scarce are perceived as more valuable. "Limited-time offers," "special issue closing soon," and "invitation-only submissions" create artificial urgency that bypasses deliberative evaluation.

**3. Social Proof (Cialdini, 2006)**: Humans infer correct behavior from observing others. Claims that "many distinguished scholars have published with us" or "we are an emerging leader in the field" provide social validation, even when unverifiable.

**4. Commitment Consistency (Cialdini, 2006)**: Once individuals make a small commitment (opening an email, reading an invitation), they experience psychological pressure to follow through with larger commitments (submitting a manuscript). Predatory invitations exploit this by minimizing initial barriers.

**5. Reciprocity Norm (Gouldner, 1960)**: Receiving unsolicited positive attention ("we admire your work") creates psychological obligation to reciprocate. Invitations frame submission as a favor to the journal rather than a service purchased by the author.

**6. Overconfidence Bias (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)**: Researchers systematically overestimate their ability to detect fraud. Even those who score well on predatory journal identification tests submit to suspect venues, believing "I can tell the difference" or "this one seems different."

These biases are universal, robust across cultures, and largely resistant to education. Knowing that scarcity bias exists does not prevent its activation. The evolutionary logic of PSO exploits this gap between explicit knowledge and implicit heuristics.

### Structural Vulnerabilities

Beyond cognitive biases, PSO exploits structural features of the academic publishing ecosystem:

**1. Information Asymmetry**: Authors have imperfect information about journal quality, acceptance rates, review rigor, indexing status, and financial stability. Legitimate journals have incentive to conceal negative information (high rejection rates, long review times); predatory journals have incentive to fabricate positive signals. Verification is costly and imperfect.

**2. Coordination Failure**: No centralized mechanism coordinates scholarly community responses. Individual researchers cannot effectively boycott predatory journals if others continue to submit. Disciplinary societies lack enforcement power. Universities cannot unilaterally change tenure criteria without risking competitive disadvantage in faculty recruitment.

**3. Metric Distortion**: Publication counts and citation metrics were originally intended as rough proxies for research quality, not as direct targets of optimization. Once embedded in formal evaluation systems (tenure reviews, grant scoring, visa requirements), they become Goodhart's Law exemplars: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure" (Goodhart, 1975). PSO emerges because the metrics can be gamed more cheaply than authentic research quality can be achieved.

**4. Regulatory Lag**: Legal and institutional systems respond slowly to novel forms of exploitation. Predatory publishing emerged in the 2000s; major institutions began addressing it only in the 2010s. By the time regulatory responses were formulated, predatory forms had evolved sophisticated countermeasures. This temporal gap between exploitation and intervention allows PSO to establish evolutionary stability.

**5. Jurisdictional Diffusion**: Predatory publishers operate across jurisdictions, frustrating regulatory enforcement. A journal may be incorporated in one country, host servers in another, employ editors in a third, and target authors globally. No single regulatory authority can effectively police this distributed system.

**6. Memetic Fitness**: In cultural evolution, ideas and institutional forms compete for adoption based on their appeal, not their truth or utility (Dawkins, 1976; Boyd & Richerson, 1985). Predatory invitations exhibit high memetic fitness: they are emotionally engaging (flattery, urgency), easy to process (clear call-to-action), and immediately rewarding (guaranteed acceptance). Legitimate editorial processes have lower memetic fitness: rejection is painful, revision is burdensome, and publication is delayed.

These structural vulnerabilities are not accidental features that could be easily corrected. They are intrinsic to large-scale, decentralized, information-intensive systems. Information asymmetry cannot be fully eliminated without prohibitive verification costs. Coordination requires collective action, which faces free-rider problems. Metrics inevitably simplify complex realities, creating gaming opportunities. Regulatory systems lag technological change by structural necessity.

PSO emerges precisely because it exploits the inevitable gaps and frictions in complex institutional systems. Understanding these mechanisms reveals why simple solutions ("just educate researchers!") fail. The problem is not individual ignorance but systemic vulnerability.

## Comparison with Alternative Theoretical Frameworks

### The Fraud Model

The standard explanation treats predatory publishing as fraud: deliberate deception by malicious actors extracting money from victims who believe they are purchasing legitimate peer review. This model predicts:

1. Detection should reduce victimization (education works)
2. Legal prosecution should deter predatory publishers
3. Blacklisting should eliminate listed journals
4. Predatory publishers should hide their identities

Empirical evidence contradicts each prediction:

1. Education has minimal effect; informed researchers still submit
2. Prosecution is rare and ineffective; publishers operate across jurisdictions
3. Blacklisted journals simply rename and re-emerge
4. Many predatory publishers openly advertise contact information, physical addresses, and ISSN numbers

The fraud model fails because it treats PSO as a principal-agent problem (publisher deceives author) rather than an equilibrium coordination problem (both parties respond rationally to distorted incentives).

### The Market Failure Model

An alternative explanation treats predatory publishing as market failure: information asymmetries prevent efficient matching between journals and authors, creating a "lemons problem" (Akerlof, 1970). Low-quality journals cannot be distinguished from high-quality ones, so average quality declines.

This model predicts:

1. Reputational mechanisms should solve the problem over time (quality journals build brands)
2. Certification intermediaries should emerge (equivalent to Consumer Reports for journals)
3. Prices should signal quality (expensive journals = legitimate; cheap = predatory)

Evidence contradicts these predictions:

1. Predatory publishing has grown despite 20+ years of reputation-building by legitimate journals
2. Certification intermediaries (DOAJ, Beall's List) have limited effect; predatory journals mimic their criteria
3. Price signals are unreliable; some legitimate open-access journals charge $3,000+ APCs while predatory journals charge $100-$400

The market failure model fails because it assumes both parties seek mutual gains from exchange. In PSO, the parties have different objectives: authors seek credentials, publishers seek revenue. The exchange occurs despite negative externalities (corrupted literature, degraded trust), which markets do not internalize.

### The Rational Choice Model

A third explanation treats participation in predatory publishing as rational choice: researchers knowingly submit to suspect venues because the benefits (publication speed, guaranteed acceptance, CV lines) outweigh costs (reputational risk, APC fees) given their constraints (time pressure, career stage, institutional requirements).

This model explains why education fails (researchers already know and choose rationally) but struggles with two anomalies:

1. Many predatory submissions come from experienced researchers at reputable institutions who do not face severe career pressure. The rational choice model predicts such researchers should avoid predatory venues.

2. The model treats reputational costs as fixed, but empirical evidence shows reputational damage is minimal when predatory submissions are widespread. This creates a coordination problem: reputation is damaged only if others refrain, but refraining is individually costly if others participate.

The rational choice model partially succeeds but lacks a dynamic evolutionary perspective. It treats payoffs as exogenous when they are actually endogenous to the institutional structure. PSO theory explains how the payoff structure itself evolves to sustain parasitic equilibria.

### Synthesizing Insights: PSO as Unified Framework

The Parasitic Spontaneous Order framework synthesizes insights from all three models while avoiding their limitations:

- **From fraud model**: Recognition that mimicry and deception are central mechanisms, but reframing deception as emergent system property rather than individual malfeasance.
- **From market failure**: Recognition that information asymmetries enable exploitation, but adding evolutionary dynamics to explain why solutions fail.
- **From rational choice**: Recognition that individuals respond optimally to incentives, but adding institutional selection to explain how those incentives are shaped by system-level evolutionary pressures.

PSO adds critical theoretical components absent from alternatives:

1. **Evolutionary perspective**: Institutions evolve; patterns that enhance their own replication out-compete welfare-enhancing alternatives.
2. **Extended phenotype**: Downstream effects (solicitations, mimicry, credential inflation) are not epiphenomena but core mechanisms of replication.
3. **Coordination equilibria**: PSO is stable not because individuals are ignorant but because unilateral deviation is penalized.
4. **Structural embeddedness**: PSO exploits cognitive biases and structural vulnerabilities that cannot be eliminated without transforming the entire institutional ecology.

This theoretical synthesis enables novel predictions (tested in Section IV) and generates policy recommendations (developed in Section V) that alternatives cannot match.
