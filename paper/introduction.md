# Introduction

## The Academic Publishing Ecosystem Under Stress

Contemporary academic publishing faces an unprecedented crisis of legitimacy. The proliferation of low-quality, predatory journals has reached epidemic proportions, with estimates ranging from 8,000 to 15,000 active predatory journals as of 2024 (Grudniewicz et al., 2019; Cobey et al., 2018). These operations exploit the open-access model, extracting article processing charges (APCs) from researchers while providing minimal or nonexistent peer review. The direct financial cost to the research community exceeds $74 million annually (Xia et al., 2015), but the true cost in degraded scientific credibility, corrupted citation networks, and eroded public trust remains incalculable.

The standard narrative treats predatory journals as aberrant actors engaged in fraud: malicious publishers who deliberately deceive researchers for profit. Policy responses follow this logic, focusing on blacklisting individual journals (Beall's List), creating whitelists of legitimate venues (Directory of Open Access Journals), and educating researchers to recognize warning signs. Yet predatory publishing continues to grow exponentially despite these interventions. The number of articles published in suspected predatory journals increased from approximately 53,000 in 2010 to over 400,000 in 2020 (Grudniewicz et al., 2019). Traditional fraud-detection frameworks cannot explain this persistent growth in the face of widespread awareness and active countermeasures.

This paper advances a fundamentally different explanation: predatory journal invitations are not mere fraud, but rather instances of *Parasitic Spontaneous Order* (PSO), a phenomenon predicted by Extended Phenotype Theory (EPT) when applied to institutional domains (Lerer, 2024a, 2024b). EPT, originally developed in evolutionary biology by Dawkins (1982), explains how genes produce effects beyond the organism's body to enhance their own replication. Applied to legal and institutional systems (Lerer, 2024a), EPT predicts that certain institutional arrangements will evolve to replicate themselves even when their effects systematically harm the individuals who produce them.

Parasitic Spontaneous Order emerges when the evolutionary logic of institutional selection produces stable coordination patterns that extract value from participants without providing commensurate benefits (Lerer, 2024b). Unlike fraud, which requires active deception by identifiable perpetrators, PSO operates through genuinely spontaneous coordination around shared incentives. Individual actors may be entirely honest within their roles while collectively generating a parasitic institutional form. The system exploits cognitive biases, information asymmetries, and coordination failures to replicate itself across domains.

## The Puzzle of Systematic Deception

The empirical phenomenon demands explanation. In a six-month period (September 2024 to February 2026), the author received eight editorial invitations ostensibly requesting manuscript submissions to academic journals. Seven of these invitations (87.5%) exhibited systematic characteristics inconsistent with legitimate editorial practice. These characteristics form a recognizable pattern:

1. **Disciplinary mismatch**: Invitations cite specific published work but request submissions to journals in unrelated fields. A paper on legal evolution theory received invitations to submit to journals in geosciences, journalism, statistics, economics, artificial intelligence, and visual reality.

2. **Mimetic legitimacy signals**: Each invitation displays professional formatting, ISSN numbers, institutional affiliations, and named editorial personnel. None exhibit obvious grammatical errors or suspicious domains. The visual presentation is indistinguishable from legitimate editorial correspondence.

3. **Emotional manipulation**: Language patterns systematically deploy urgency ("limited time offer"), flattery ("we are impressed by your significant contributions"), scarcity ("special issue closing soon"), and authority cues ("editorial board invitation").

4. **Friction reduction**: Submission mechanisms bypass standard editorial management systems (OJS, ScholarOne, Manuscript Central). Authors are invited to submit via direct email attachment, minimizing barriers to participation.

5. **Economic opacity**: Article processing charges (when disclosed) appear only after submission. One invitation disclosed a $198 USD fee; others provided no pricing information in the initial contact.

6. **Automation indicators**: Multiple invitations arrive simultaneously from journals with no topical overlap. Title scraping is exact; abstracts are quoted verbatim. Email addresses receive invitations for papers published under institutional affiliations with no public email listing.

These patterns are not random. They represent a coherent strategic response to the institutional environment of academic publishing. Yet standard fraud explanations face three critical problems:

**Problem 1: Coordination Without Conspiracy**. The invitations exhibit remarkable similarity in structure, timing, and rhetorical patterns despite originating from nominally independent publishers (Academix, Opast, Glint Open Access, Academic Vision, Premier Publishers, Explore Science, OASK). Standard fraud models assume coordination requires communication among fraudsters. But no evidence suggests these entities coordinate their activities. How does such systematic similarity emerge without explicit coordination?

**Problem 2: Rational Response by Targets**. Many predatory journals succeed in attracting submissions despite widespread awareness of the problem. Researchers knowingly submit to suspect venues for rational reasons: publication speed, guaranteed acceptance, resume padding, institutional publication requirements, and visa eligibility criteria (Cobey et al., 2018; Kurt, 2018). If the journals provide these services and researchers benefit from them, in what sense is the transaction fraudulent? Both parties receive what they seek.

**Problem 3: Evolutionary Persistence**. Blacklisting efforts have proven ineffective. When Beall's List was active (2008-2017), predatory publishers responded by creating new journals faster than existing venues could be documented (Beall, 2015). After the list was taken down due to legal threats, predatory publishing accelerated. Whitelisting (DOAJ) creates incentives for predatory journals to mimic legitimacy signals without changing substantive practices. Educational interventions show minimal effect; researchers who score well on predatory journal identification tests still submit to such venues (Shamseer et al., 2017). Why does predatory publishing exhibit such robust evolutionary persistence?

## Theoretical Innovation: From Individual Deception to Institutional Selection

This paper resolves these puzzles by reframing predatory journal invitations through Extended Phenotype Theory. The key theoretical move is recognizing that academic publishing operates as an evolutionary system subject to institutional selection pressures. Just as biological evolution selects for genes that maximize their own replication (not necessarily organismal fitness), institutional evolution selects for patterns that enhance their own persistence (not necessarily social welfare).

Predatory journal invitations represent an extended phenotype: the downstream causal effects produced by the institutional logic of publication-count metrics, open-access business models, and disciplinary fragmentation. The "organism" is not any individual publisher, editor, or author, but rather the *institutional form itself*, the self-perpetuating pattern of parasitic academic publishing. This pattern replicates by exploiting cognitive biases (authority, scarcity, social proof), structural vulnerabilities (information asymmetry, coordination failure), and evolutionary pressures (publish-or-perish incentives).

The concept of Parasitic Spontaneous Order distinguishes this phenomenon from both fraud and legitimate market activity. Unlike fraud, PSO does not require intentional deception. Individual actors may be sincere; the parasitic character emerges from the system-level dynamics. Unlike legitimate markets, PSO does not generate mutual gains from exchange. One party (publishers) extracts value while providing minimal substantive service; the other party (authors) participates due to distorted incentives created by external institutions (universities, funding agencies, visa authorities).

The PSO framework generates testable predictions:

1. **Pattern consistency**: Parasitic invitations should exhibit recognizable similarities across independent publishers because they face similar selection pressures.

2. **Evolutionary adaptation**: As countermeasures emerge (blacklists, education), parasitic forms should adapt by mimicking legitimacy signals more closely.

3. **Cross-domain generalization**: The same institutional logic should produce similar parasitic forms wherever analogous selection pressures exist (conference invitations, editorial board recruitment, citation manipulation services).

4. **Resistance to elimination**: Interventions targeting individual actors (shutting down specific journals) should prove ineffective because the institutional form will re-emerge through different actors.

## Empirical Contribution and Methodological Approach

This paper makes several empirical contributions. First, it presents a systematic corpus of eight editorial invitations received by a single researcher, documented in real time with complete metadata (sender information, timestamps, referenced works, submission procedures). This corpus provides a controlled natural experiment: invitations referencing identical source material (a published paper on legal evolution theory) but varying in legitimacy, allowing direct comparison of patterns.

Second, the paper develops a quantitative Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO) Index measuring six theoretically derived dimensions: disciplinary mismatch, mimetic legitimacy, emotional hooks, friction reduction, economic opacity, and automation indicators. This index achieves 100% classification accuracy in the corpus (8/8 cases correctly identified), distinguishing the single legitimate invitation from seven parasitic cases with no false positives or false negables.

Third, the paper validates the framework through a natural experiment: the case of Brazilian academic Marcia Aparecida Diago, who created a fabricated persona with plagiarized publications and received editorial invitations, board appointments, and reviewer requests despite producing no original research (supplementary materials). This case confirms the prediction that parasitic systems target reputational signals (publication records, institutional affiliations) rather than substantive scholarly contributions.

Fourth, the paper stress-tests the framework against ten theoretical objections, demonstrating robustness across alternative explanations (genuine editorial interest, market inefficiency, rational exchange, cultural differences, nascent journal legitimacy). The PSO model out-explains competing hypotheses by accounting for pattern consistency, evolutionary persistence, cross-domain generalization, and selection pressure dynamics.

Fifth, the paper extends the analysis to seven related domains (academic conferences, editorial board invitations, citation manipulation services, reviewer recruitment, special issue solicitations, book chapter invitations, peer review platforms), documenting how the same institutional logic produces similar parasitic forms across contexts.

## Normative and Policy Implications

The reframing from fraud to Parasitic Spontaneous Order has profound policy implications. Fraud-based interventions (blacklisting, prosecution, education) treat the symptoms rather than the underlying institutional dynamics. Such interventions can even accelerate PSO evolution by creating selection pressure for more sophisticated mimicry.

Effective countermeasures must target the selection pressures that make parasitic forms viable:

1. **Metric reform**: Replace publication-count incentives with quality-weighted assessments, removing the demand-side pressure that sustains predatory venues.

2. **Transparency mandates**: Require disclosure of acceptance rates, review timelines, APC structures, and editorial board qualifications before submission, eliminating information asymmetries.

3. **Structural interventions**: Create institutional buffers that break the direct link between publication counts and career outcomes (tenure, visa eligibility, grant funding), reducing pressure to game metrics.

4. **Collective action**: Establish discipline-specific consortia to certify legitimate venues and coordinate rejection of parasitic submissions, solving the coordination problem that allows PSO to persist.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section II develops the theoretical framework, explaining Extended Phenotype Theory, Parasitic Spontaneous Order, and their application to academic publishing. Section III presents the methodology, describing the corpus construction, PSO Index development, and validation procedures. Section IV reports results, documenting the six systematic patterns observed across seven parasitic invitations. Section V discusses theoretical implications, addresses objections, and extends the framework to related domains. Section VI concludes with policy recommendations and directions for future research.

The stakes extend beyond academic publishing. If institutional arrangements can evolve parasitic spontaneous orders that extract value while mimicking legitimacy, then similar dynamics likely operate in financial regulation (Lerer, 2024b), legal complexity (Lerer, 2024a), healthcare administration, educational credentialing, and professional licensing. Understanding the evolutionary logic of parasitic institutions is essential for designing governance systems that resist colonization by value-extracting forms.
