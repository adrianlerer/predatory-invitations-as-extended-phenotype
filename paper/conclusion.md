# Conclusion

## Summary of Core Findings

This paper reframes predatory academic publishing through the lens of Extended Phenotype Theory and the concept of Parasitic Spontaneous Order. The core empirical finding is clear: seven of eight editorial invitations (87.5%) received by a single researcher over 18 months exhibited systematic characteristics inconsistent with legitimate editorial practice. These invitations were not random fraud but structured institutional adaptations selected for their effectiveness in exploiting cognitive biases and structural vulnerabilities in the academic publishing ecosystem.

The six-dimension Parasitic Spontaneous Order Index achieved 100% classification accuracy in distinguishing parasitic from legitimate invitations. Disciplinary mismatch emerged as the strongest discriminator: 85.7% of parasitic invitations solicited submissions to journals in unrelated fields, revealing automated database scraping rather than substantive editorial evaluation. Emotional manipulation tactics appeared in 100% of parasitic cases, targeting authority bias, scarcity bias, and reciprocity norms. All parasitic invitations reduced submission friction by bypassing editorial management systems, promising rapid review, and minimizing formatting requirements. Economic opacity (85.7% failed to disclose APCs upfront) exploited sunk cost bias. Automation indicators (template language, batch timing, exact title scraping) confirmed scalable, low-cost operations.

Remarkably, these patterns exhibited high consistency across seven nominally independent publishers (coefficient of variation 0.06), despite no evidence of overt coordination. This pattern consistency without conspiracy strongly supports the spontaneous order interpretation: similar selection pressures produce convergent institutional evolution. Publishers independently discover that certain tactics (disciplinary indifference, emotional manipulation, friction reduction, economic opacity) enhance manuscript acquisition, and these tactics are selected for through competitive market dynamics.

The legitimate control case (Journal of Computational Law and Legal Technology) scored 0.08 on the PSO Index, exhibiting strong disciplinary match, emotionally neutral language, standard editorial procedures, transparent pricing, and individualized outreach. The gap between the legitimate case and the mean parasitic case (0.88) is 0.80 points, indicating robust discrimination.

External validation through the Diago case (supplementary materials) confirmed theoretical predictions: a fabricated scholar persona with plagiarized publications received editorial invitations, board appointments, and reviewer requests, demonstrating that parasitic systems target reputational signals rather than substantive contributions. Extensions to related domains (conference invitations, editorial board recruitment, citation manipulation services) exhibit identical PSO characteristics, supporting cross-domain generalizability.

## Theoretical Contributions

This paper makes four significant theoretical contributions:

### 1. Extended Phenotype Theory Applied to Institutions

The paper demonstrates that Extended Phenotype Theory (EPT), originally formulated to explain biological evolution, successfully predicts institutional patterns in cultural and organizational domains. The key insight is recognizing that **institutional forms are replicators** subject to selection based on their downstream effects. Predatory journal invitations are extended phenotypes: causal effects produced by the institutional form that enhance its own replication. Just as beaver dams are extended phenotypes of beaver genes (effects that enhance gene replication), predatory invitations are extended phenotypes of the parasitic publishing model (effects that enhance institutional replication).

This is not mere metaphor. The formal structure of EPT (replicators, variation, selection) applies wherever these conditions obtain. Academic publishing manifestly exhibits evolutionary dynamics: institutional forms vary, selection operates based on replicative success (submission rates, revenue, persistence), and successful forms replicate through imitation and resource flows. The theoretical generalization from biology to institutions is rigorous, not analogical.

The extension challenges conventional principal-agent frameworks that treat organizational pathology as individual malfeasance. EPT reveals that institutional forms can evolve to harm their participants while enhancing their own replication, regardless of individual intentions. Understanding this evolutionary logic is essential for designing interventions that target structural selection pressures rather than merely sanctioning individual actors.

### 2. Parasitic Spontaneous Order as New Concept

The paper introduces Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO) as a general category of institutional pathology. PSO describes coordination patterns that emerge without central planning (spontaneous), exhibit self-reinforcing dynamics (stable equilibria), extract value asymmetrically (parasitic), replicate through extended phenotypic effects (evolutionarily fit), mimic beneficial institutions (deceptive), and resist elimination despite recognition (evolutionarily stable).

This concept synthesizes insights from multiple frameworks. From Austrian economics, it borrows the concept of spontaneous order but recognizes that not all spontaneous orders are welfare-enhancing. From evolutionary game theory, it borrows the concept of evolutionarily stable strategies but applies it to institutional forms rather than organism behaviors. From institutional economics, it borrows the concept of path dependency and equilibrium multiplicity but adds explicit evolutionary dynamics.

PSO is deliberately narrow to maintain analytical precision. Not every harmful institution is PSO; some may be designed coercion (not spontaneous) or tragic dilemmas with mutual gains unrealized (not parasitic extraction). PSO specifically identifies systems where coordination emerges without conspiracy, one party extracts value without providing commensurate benefit, and the pattern persists through evolutionary stability.

The concept enables cross-domain analysis. Predatory publishing, payday lending, regulatory capture, credential inflation, citation manipulation, and astroturfing exhibit PSO characteristics despite operating in different institutional contexts. Understanding their shared evolutionary logic enables policy learning across domains.

### 3. Cognitive and Structural Mechanisms

The paper identifies specific mechanisms through which PSO operates:

**Cognitive mechanisms**: Parasitic invitations systematically exploit six cognitive biases documented in behavioral economics: authority bias (deference to editorial titles), scarcity bias (artificial urgency), social proof (claims of widespread participation), commitment consistency (small initial commitments escalate), reciprocity norm (unsolicited praise creates obligation), and overconfidence bias (researchers overestimate their detection ability).

These biases are universal, robust across cultures, and resistant to education. Knowing that scarcity bias exists does not prevent its activation. PSO succeeds precisely because it exploits the gap between explicit knowledge (researchers know about predatory journals) and implicit heuristics (emotional responses to flattery and urgency).

**Structural mechanisms**: PSO exploits five structural vulnerabilities: information asymmetry (authors cannot easily verify journal quality), coordination failure (no mechanism coordinates collective rejection), metric distortion (publication counts become optimization targets), regulatory lag (legal systems respond slowly), and jurisdictional diffusion (publishers operate across borders).

These vulnerabilities are intrinsic to large-scale, decentralized, information-intensive systems. Complete elimination is impossible without prohibitive costs. PSO emerges precisely because it exploits inevitable gaps in complex institutional systems.

Understanding these mechanisms explains why simple interventions (education, blacklisting) fail. Education does not eliminate cognitive biases; blacklisting does not eliminate structural vulnerabilities. Effective intervention requires changing the institutional environment that makes these mechanisms exploitable.

### 4. Evolutionary Stability Analysis

The paper demonstrates that predatory publishing is an Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS) in the formal game-theoretic sense. Parasitic publishers out-compete legitimate journals regardless of opponent strategy because they have cost advantages (no substantive peer review), revenue advantages (APCs per article), and market segmentation (target authors excluded from legitimate venues). No individual actor can profitably deviate from the parasitic equilibrium without coordinated collective action.

This evolutionary stability explains why predatory publishing persists despite 20+ years of awareness, countermeasures, and public condemnation. The problem is not ignorance, lack of enforcement, or individual moral failure. The problem is that the institutional environment creates selection pressures that favor parasitic forms. Until those selection pressures change, parasitic publishing will persist regardless of interventions targeting individual publishers or researchers.

The evolutionary framework reframes policy analysis. Instead of asking "how do we punish predatory publishers?" or "how do we educate researchers?", the relevant question becomes "how do we change selection pressures to make parasitic strategies non-viable?" This shift in perspective generates fundamentally different policy recommendations.

## Policy Implications

Standard interventions (blacklisting, education, voluntary codes) fail because they target symptoms rather than structural causes. The evolutionary stability of PSO requires interventions that change the institutional environment across four dimensions:

**1. Metric reform**: Replace publication-count incentives with quality-weighted assessments. Eliminate count-based thresholds from tenure rubrics, grant applications, and visa criteria. Use narrative evaluation of contribution rather than quantitative tallies. This removes the demand-side pressure that sustains predatory publishing.

**2. Transparency mandates**: Require journals to disclose acceptance rates, review timelines, APC structures, and editorial board qualifications before submission. Create public registries of editorial practices. Verify editor credentials through institutional email. Mandatory transparency reduces mimicry effectiveness and shifts competitive advantage toward genuinely selective journals.

**3. Collective action coordination**: Form disciplinary consortia to certify legitimate venues and coordinate rejection of parasitic submissions. Create university coalitions adopting uniform standards. Coordinate funding agency policies excluding predatory publications from grant CVs. Align visa authority criteria across countries. Coordinated action solves free-rider problems that prevent voluntary boycotts.

**4. Structural buffers**: Extend tenure clocks to reduce time pressure. Create alternative career pathways with different evaluation criteria. Decouple grant funding from recent publication velocity. Base academic visas on institutional sponsorship rather than individual publication metrics. Structural buffers reduce the desperation that drives predatory submission.

No single intervention suffices. Blacklisting without metric reform simply displaces the problem (new journals emerge to serve persistent demand). Education without transparency mandates cannot overcome information asymmetry. Voluntary pledges without coordination mechanisms face free-rider problems. Effective policy requires simultaneous intervention across multiple levels to shift the evolutionary equilibrium from parasitic to legitimate.

The analogy to public health is instructive: eliminating infectious disease requires not just treating sick individuals but changing environmental conditions (sanitation, vaccination, vector control). Similarly, eliminating parasitic publishing requires not just shutting down individual journals but changing institutional conditions (metric systems, transparency, coordination) that allow parasitic forms to thrive.

## Broader Implications for Institutional Design

The implications extend beyond academic publishing. If institutional forms can evolve parasitic spontaneous orders that extract value while mimicking legitimacy, then similar dynamics likely operate in financial regulation, legal complexity, healthcare administration, educational credentialing, and professional licensing. Every domain with information asymmetries, coordination failures, and metric-driven evaluation faces PSO risk.

This recognition transforms institutional design principles. Traditional institutional design focuses on aligning individual incentives (mechanism design) or preventing individual malfeasance (compliance and enforcement). The PSO framework reveals a deeper challenge: **institutional environments can select for pathological forms regardless of individual intentions**. Well-meaning actors can collectively produce parasitic outcomes because the institutional structure rewards extraction-maximizing strategies.

Designing PSO-resistant institutions requires:

**1. Anticipatory selection analysis**: Before implementing institutional changes (new metrics, evaluation systems, regulatory frameworks), analyze what forms those changes will select for. If institutional forms that game the system replicate more successfully than forms that achieve substantive goals, the system will evolve toward gaming.

**2. Mimicry-proofing**: Design certification systems that are costly for parasitic forms to mimic. Surface-level legitimacy signals (ISSN numbers, editorial boards, professional websites) are easily copied. Substantive characteristics (disciplinary expertise, rigorous review, selective acceptance) are costly to fake. Certification should target characteristics that exhibit high separation between genuine and parasitic forms.

**3. Evolutionary monitoring**: Monitor institutional evolution over time to detect early emergence of parasitic forms. Rapid growth, unexpected pattern consistency across nominally independent actors, and mimetic sophistication are warning signs. Early detection enables intervention before evolutionary stability is established.

**4. Coordination architecture**: Build coordination mechanisms into institutional design from the outset. Collective action against parasitic forms requires infrastructure (information sharing, coordinated decision-making, enforcement mechanisms). Designing this infrastructure prospectively is easier than retrofitting after parasitic equilibria are established.

**5. Metric skepticism**: Treat all metrics as vulnerable to gaming. Goodhart's Law ("when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure") is not occasional failure but systematic risk. Multiple metrics, qualitative evaluation, and periodic metric rotation reduce gaming vulnerability.

These principles apply broadly. Financial regulators should anticipate that new rules will select for forms that exploit loopholes. Education reformers should anticipate that standardized tests will select for teaching-to-the-test. Healthcare policymakers should anticipate that quality metrics will select for metric gaming. The PSO framework provides conceptual tools for this anticipatory analysis.

## Future Research Directions

This paper opens several research trajectories:

**Empirical expansion**: Compile large-scale corpora of predatory invitations across fields, countries, and career stages. Use machine learning to automate PSO Index scoring. Conduct field experiments with fabricated scholar profiles to test targeting strategies. Document evolutionary history of predatory tactics over decades.

**Theoretical development**: Formal agent-based models simulating academic publishing ecosystems under different institutional conditions. Derive necessary and sufficient conditions for PSO emergence. Extend game-theoretic analysis to multi-level selection (individual, institutional, systemic). Specify boundary conditions distinguishing PSO from other forms of institutional pathology.

**Cross-domain validation**: Apply PSO framework to payday lending, regulatory capture, credential inflation, and other domains. Test whether same mechanisms operate across contexts. Identify domain-specific vs. universal features. Develop domain-specific intervention strategies based on shared evolutionary logic.

**Intervention evaluation**: Partner with universities, funding agencies, or countries to pilot structural interventions. Use randomized controlled trials or quasi-experimental designs. Measure causal effects on predatory publishing prevalence. Document unintended consequences and adaptation responses.

**Comparative institutional analysis**: Compare countries with high vs. low predatory publishing rates. Identify institutional differences (metric systems, tenure structures, regulatory approaches) that predict outcomes. Test whether successful models transfer across contexts.

**Cognitive mechanism isolation**: Experimental studies isolating effects of individual invitation characteristics. Which cognitive biases are most exploitable? Which interventions (warning labels, decision aids, cooling-off periods) reduce vulnerability? How do individual differences (cognitive reflection, risk aversion, career pressure) moderate susceptibility?

This research agenda transforms predatory publishing from practical nuisance into model system for understanding institutional evolution, parasitic spontaneous order, and regulatory intervention dynamics. The insights gained will generalize to other domains exhibiting similar pathologies.

## Closing Reflection

This paper began with a puzzle: why do predatory journal invitations exhibit such striking similarity despite originating from nominally independent publishers? Why do interventions consistently fail despite widespread awareness? Why does the problem intensify rather than resolve over time?

The answer lies in evolutionary dynamics. Predatory publishing is not fraud in the conventional sense (individual deception for profit) but rather an extended phenotype shaped by institutional selection. The invitations are downstream causal effects that enhance the institutional form's replication. The similarity without conspiracy reflects convergent evolution under similar selection pressures. The intervention failures reflect attempts to eliminate symptoms while structural causes persist. The temporal intensification reflects evolutionary adaptation, with parasitic forms becoming more sophisticated in response to detection mechanisms.

Understanding predatory publishing as Parasitic Spontaneous Order does not excuse individual publishers, absolve researchers who knowingly participate, or dismiss the serious harm to scholarly integrity. But it does explain why moral condemnation and punitive enforcement prove insufficient. The institutional form persists not through conspiracy but through evolutionary stability. It replicates not through deliberate coordination but through spontaneous order. It adapts not through strategic planning but through selection of variation.

This evolutionary perspective is simultaneously pessimistic and optimistic. Pessimistic because simple solutions (education, blacklisting, voluntary compliance) cannot overcome evolutionary stability. The problem is deeper and more structural than conventional analyses recognize. Optimistic because evolutionary theory provides precise predictions about which interventions will succeed: those that change selection pressures.

If publication-count metrics create demand for parasitic venues, metric reform eliminates that demand. If information asymmetry enables mimicry, transparency mandates reduce mimicry effectiveness. If coordination failures prevent collective responses, coordination architecture enables collective action. If structural pressures drive desperate submission, structural buffers reduce that desperation. These interventions target the evolutionary environment, not individual actors, and thereby address root causes rather than symptoms.

The theoretical framework developed here applies beyond academic publishing. Wherever institutional forms compete for resources and survival, wherever information asymmetries enable mimicry, wherever coordination failures prevent collective responses, wherever metrics distort incentives, Parasitic Spontaneous Order threatens to emerge. Financial markets, regulatory systems, professional credentialing, educational assessment, healthcare administration, and legal complexity all exhibit structural vulnerabilities to PSO.

The challenge for institutional design in the 21st century is anticipating these evolutionary dynamics and building institutions resistant to colonization by parasitic forms. This requires moving beyond naive mechanism design (assuming actors will behave as intended) toward evolutionary institutional design (anticipating what forms the system will select for). It requires recognizing that institutions are not static rules but evolving ecosystems subject to selection pressures. And it requires accepting that vigilance against institutional parasitism is not a one-time fix but an ongoing evolutionary struggle.

The predatory journal invitations documented in this study are not isolated irritants but harbingers of a broader institutional challenge: how to design governance systems that resist evolutionary emergence of value-extracting forms. Meeting that challenge requires theoretical frameworks that take evolutionary dynamics seriously. This paper offers one such framework. Its empirical validation in the academic publishing context suggests broader applicability. Whether humanity succeeds in designing PSO-resistant institutions may determine whether 21st-century governance systems serve human flourishing or merely perpetuate sophisticated extraction.

The stakes are high. Academic publishing is humanity's system for producing and validating knowledge. If that system can be colonized by parasitic forms that extract resources while corrupting the literature, what other systems face similar vulnerabilities? Financial regulation shapes global economic stability. Legal systems determine justice and liberty. Healthcare systems govern life and death. Educational credentialing allocates opportunity. If these domains exhibit the same evolutionary dynamics as academic publishing, then understanding and controlling Parasitic Spontaneous Order is not merely an academic exercise but an existential challenge for institutional governance.

This paper provides conceptual tools for that challenge. Whether those tools prove sufficient depends on humanity's willingness to confront evolutionary realities and redesign institutions accordingly. The alternative is allowing parasitic forms to proliferate until the costs become catastrophic. The choice is ours. But the evolutionary dynamics are not.
