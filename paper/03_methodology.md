# 3. Methodology: Corpus Construction and PSO Index Development

## 3.1 Research Design: Naturalistic Observation + Diagnostic Tool Development

This study combines two methodological strategies:

1. **Naturalistic corpus analysis:** Systematic documentation of invitation emails received by the author over a six-week period (October 15 – December 5, 2025). This is **not** an experimental intervention but observation of naturally occurring phenomena.

2. **Diagnostic tool development:** Construction of the **Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO) Index**, a quantitative instrument that operationalizes the theoretical framework into a 0–5 scale applicable to any journal invitation.

The research design follows **theory-driven qualitative analysis** (Glaser & Strauss 1967; Miles & Huberman 1994): theoretical predictions guide pattern identification, but patterns are inductively refined through iterative coding. The PSO Index is then validated through (a) internal consistency (patterns cluster in predicted ways) and (b) external validation (Diago case confirms predictions).

### 3.1.1 Epistemological Position: Critical Realism

This paper adopts a **critical realist** epistemology (Bhaskar 1978): social phenomena exhibit real causal mechanisms that exist independently of observation but are mediated by context. The EPT+PSO framework posits a **real mechanism** (selection pressures on journal entities), observable through **empirical patterns** (mimicry, disciplinary mismatch, content irrelevance).

This contrasts with:

- **Naive realism:** Assumes observed patterns directly reveal mechanisms (neglects selection bias, confounding).
- **Social constructivism:** Denies objective mechanisms, emphasizes subjective interpretation (cannot explain stability, predictability).

Critical realism justifies **small-N, theory-driven research**: the goal is not statistical generalization (which requires representative sampling) but **mechanistic explanation** (which requires theory validation through precise observation). The eight-case corpus is sufficient to test whether the EPT+PSO framework explains observed patterns better than alternative theories (fraud, principal-agent, information asymmetry).

## 3.2 Corpus Construction: Sampling and Selection

### 3.2.1 Sampling Frame

The corpus consists of **all invitation emails** received by the author between October 15 and December 5, 2025, that:

1. **Cited a specific SSRN publication** by name (not generic "we invite submissions")
2. **Invited manuscript submission** to a journal
3. **Arrived in author's primary professional email** (not spam folder filtering)

**Included:** 8 invitations meeting criteria  
**Excluded:** Generic journal announcements (n≈20), conference invitations (n≈10), invitations not citing author work (n≈15)

**Key selection feature:** This is a **convenience sample** but is **information-rich** (Patton 2002): it includes **one legitimate control case** (Case #1, JCLLT) and **seven cases exhibiting classic predatory patterns**, enabling systematic comparison.

### 3.2.2 Author Characteristics: Selection Bias

The corpus reflects the author's **specific profile**, which introduces systematic bias:

**Author characteristics:**
- **Seniority:** 30+ years professional experience, visible SSRN presence
- **Discipline:** Law, corporate governance, legal theory
- **Specialization:** Interdisciplinary (law + evolutionary biology, law + institutional economics)
- **Publication venue:** SSRN preprint server (high visibility, open scraping access)
- **Language:** English

**Bias implications:**

1. **High visibility:** Senior scholars with established SSRN profiles likely receive **more** invitations than early-career researchers. Frequency observed here may overestimate general prevalence.

2. **Interdisciplinary vulnerability:** Papers bridging law and biology may trigger **automated mismatch** more often than single-discipline work. Mismatch rate may be inflated.

3. **Geographic bias:** Author based in Argentina but publishes in English; invitations may reflect global scraping patterns that differ from region-specific targeting.

4. **Platform bias:** SSRN is heavily scraped by PSO entities (no paywall, full-text access). Researchers using institutional repositories or ResearchGate may experience different patterns.

**Mitigation:** Results are presented as **case studies**, not prevalence estimates. The PSO Index is a **diagnostic tool** for individual invitations, not a population statistic. Generalizability depends on independent replication across disciplines, career stages, and platforms.

### 3.2.3 Temporal Window: External Validity

The October–December 2025 window coincides with:

1. **Post-COVID academic acceleration:** Increased pressure to publish (hiring cycles resuming, backlog clearing).
2. **SSRN paper visibility:** Author's papers on Extended Phenotype Theory of Law (uploaded mid-2024) gaining traction.
3. **Increased PSO sophistication:** Mimicry tactics evolved since Beall's List shutdown (2017).

This timing may **inflate** PSO prevalence relative to earlier periods (less pressure) or later periods (potential regulatory responses). However, EPT+PSO framework predicts **persistence**: as long as underlying incentives (metric-driven evaluation) remain, PSO should remain stable.

## 3.3 Data Collection and Documentation

### 3.3.1 Email Capture Protocol

Each invitation email was:

1. **Saved in raw form:** Full headers, sender information, body text preserved in plaintext (.txt) format.
2. **Anonymized:** Personal identifiers (author email addresses) redacted in public documentation.
3. **Timestamped:** Date received recorded.
4. **Categorized:** Preliminary classification (legitimate / suspicious) noted at receipt.

**No selection manipulation:** All invitations meeting inclusion criteria (cited author work, invited submission) were included. No cherry-picking of extreme cases.

### 3.3.2 Structured Coding Form

Each case was coded using a **six-dimension framework** derived from EPT+PSO theory:

| **Dimension** | **Definition** | **Indicators (Binary: 0/1)** |
|---------------|----------------|------------------------------|
| **D1: Disciplinary Mismatch** | Journal's claimed field vs. author manuscript field | 0 = Alignment; 1 = Dramatic mismatch |
| **D2: Mimetic Legitimacy** | Replication of institutional signals | 0 = Minimal signals; 1 = ISSN, DOI, editorial board claims |
| **D3: Emotional Hooks** | Manipulation tactics (urgency, flattery, fear) | 0 = Neutral tone; 1 = Urgency/flattery/FOMO |
| **D4: Friction Minimization** | Reduction of submission barriers | 0 = Standard process; 1 = Email attachment, rapid decision |
| **D5: Automation Indicators** | Evidence of scraping/template use | 0 = Personalized; 1 = Template errors, generic text |
| **D6: Financial Extraction** | Explicit APC disclosure and emphasis | 0 = No APC / transparent; 1 = APC disclosed + emphasized |

**Coding procedure:**

1. **Independent coding:** Author coded all cases independently using above framework.
2. **Verification:** Two external reviewers (academic colleagues familiar with publishing) blind-coded 50% of cases (n=4) to assess reliability.
3. **Inter-rater reliability:** Cohen's kappa = 0.92 (excellent agreement; Landis & Koch 1977).
4. **Discrepancies:** Resolved through discussion and consensus.

**Binary scoring rationale:** Each dimension is scored 0 (absent/low) or 1 (present/high) to maximize objectivity and reproducibility. This sacrifices granularity but gains **practitioner usability**: researchers can apply the index in real-time without extensive training.

### 3.3.3 PSO Index Construction

The **PSO Index** aggregates five dimensions (D1–D5) into a 0–5 scale:

**PSO Score = D1 + D2 + D3 + D4 + D5**

**Dimension 6 (Financial Extraction) excluded from index** because:

1. Many predatory journals **do not disclose APC** in initial invitations (to avoid immediate rejection).
2. Some legitimate journals **do charge APCs** (PLOS, BMC, Frontiers).
3. APC presence is **insufficient** for PSO classification; **context matters** (transparent vs. concealed, emphasized vs. mentioned).

D6 is documented **separately** as supplementary evidence but not scored.

**Classification thresholds:**

- **PSO Score 0–1:** Legitimate (low parasitic signal)
- **PSO Score 2–3:** Ambiguous (requires further verification)
- **PSO Score 4–5:** PSO (high parasitic signal)

**Rationale for thresholds:**

- **Score 0–1:** At most one red flag. Could reflect emerging journals (Case #1 scored 0 on mismatch but 1 on emotional hooks due to enthusiasm; total 2.0 but legitimacy confirmed through external verification).
- **Score 4–5:** Four or five red flags. Strong convergent evidence of PSO.
- **Score 2–3:** Uncertain. Requires manual investigation (website quality, editorial board verification, indexing status).

### 3.3.4 Data Structure: Multi-Level Documentation

Each case is documented at **three levels:**

**Level 1: Quantitative summary (CSV file)**  
`data/invitations_corpus.csv`: Case ID, journal name, publisher, sender, discipline claimed, discipline actual, ISSN, date, PSO score.

`data/pso_index_scores.csv`: Case ID, five dimensional scores, total PSO score, classification.

**Level 2: Qualitative narrative (Markdown file)**  
`emails/email_XX_[name].md`: Full email text, context, analysis, verification attempts, author decision.

**Level 3: Theoretical integration (Supplementary documents)**  
`supplementary/case_study_diago.md`: External validation case.  
`supplementary/stress_test_cases.md`: Theoretical objections and responses.

This **triangulation** (Denzin 1978) allows readers to assess:

1. **Quantitative patterns:** Do PSO scores cluster as predicted?
2. **Qualitative richness:** What specific language, tactics, and signals appear?
3. **Theoretical coherence:** Do cases align with EPT+PSO predictions?

## 3.4 Validation Strategy: Internal + External

### 3.4.1 Internal Validation: Control Case Contrast

**Case #1 (JCLLT)** functions as a **negative control**: a legitimate new journal that should score **low** on PSO dimensions. This tests whether the index **distinguishes** legitimate from parasitic entities.

**Predicted pattern:**
- JCLLT should score 0–2 (low PSO signal)
- Other cases should score 4–5 (high PSO signal)
- **No false positives** (legitimate journals misclassified as PSO)

**Observed pattern (Section 4):**
- JCLLT: PSO Score = 2.0 (D2=1 [mimicry], D3=1 [enthusiasm], others=0)
- Classification: **LEGITIMATE** (confirmed through external verification: editor credentials, OASPA membership, transparent policies)
- Six cases: PSO Score = 5.0 (all dimensions present)
- One case: PSO Score = 5.0 (all dimensions present)

**Interpretation:** Index successfully distinguishes legitimate (score 2, below threshold 4) from PSO (score 5, above threshold 4). Zero false positives in this corpus.

### 3.4.2 External Validation: Diago Case

The **Diago case study** (Appendix A) provides **independent confirmation** of EPT+PSO Prediction 2 (content irrelevance):

**Design:**
1. Researcher (Diago, pseudonymized) submitted an **intentionally absurd** paper to a journal scoring 5/5 on PSO Index.
2. Paper contained mathematical errors, nonsensical claims, contradictory arguments.
3. Hypothesis: If journal functions as credential vendor (PSO), paper is accepted without substantive review.

**Result:**
- Paper **accepted** within 48 hours
- **No substantive review** (only formatting requests)
- APC requested (USD 150)

**Interpretation:** Confirms functional divergence. Journal's claimed function (quality validation) entirely absent. Content irrelevant.

This case was **not part of the corpus** (different author, different journal) but validates the framework's **predictive power**: PSO Index correctly identified a journal that operates as credential vendor.

### 3.4.3 Stress Testing: Theoretical Robustness

Ten objections to the EPT+PSO framework are systematically addressed in Appendix B:

1. "It's just fraud, not spontaneous order" → Legality + decentralization distinguish PSO
2. "This is just principal-agent problem" → Triadic structure, emergence differ
3. "Cynical about new journals" → Case #1 proves selectivity
4. "Incompetence, not parasitism" → Stability contradicts incompetence
5. "Applies to all journals" → Functional divergence distinguishes
6. "Authors are complicit" → EPT explains without requiring awareness
7. "Regulatory capture explains better" → Wrong mechanism (no regulator)
8. "Just a metaphor" → Falsifiable predictions make it mechanistic
9. "Just ban them" → Doesn't address underlying incentives
10. "Evolution doesn't apply to culture" → Cultural evolution is real

**Outcome:** Framework survives stress testing. Objections either mischaracterize PSO (conflate with fraud, assume conspiracy) or fail to account for stability, mimicry, and content irrelevance.

## 3.5 Analytical Approach: Abductive Reasoning

The analytical strategy follows **abductive logic** (Peirce 1903; Reichertz 2007):

1. **Observation:** Puzzling patterns (disciplinary mismatch, template errors, rapid acceptance)
2. **Theory generation:** EPT+PSO framework proposed as explanation
3. **Prediction derivation:** Framework implies mimicry increase, content irrelevance, mismatch stability
4. **Empirical testing:** Predictions tested against corpus + Diago case
5. **Theory refinement:** Stress testing addresses objections, strengthens framework

This contrasts with:

- **Deduction:** Start with theory, test hypotheses. (Problem: No prior theory of parasitic publishing as EPT)
- **Induction:** Observe patterns, generalize. (Problem: Cannot explain **why** patterns persist)

Abduction integrates **pattern recognition** (what do we observe?) with **mechanistic explanation** (why does it occur?). The EPT+PSO framework is not merely descriptive but **explanatory**: it accounts for stability (ESS), diversification (selection on mimicry), and content irrelevance (functional divergence).

## 3.6 Ethical Considerations

### 3.6.1 No Human Subjects Research

This study analyzes **unsolicited emails** received by the author. No experimental manipulation, no participant recruitment, no deception. Institutional Review Board (IRB) exemption applies (analysis of publicly available communications not involving intervention).

### 3.6.2 Anonymization of Senders

Sender names and email addresses in public-facing documentation are **real but not personally identifiable beyond what appears in original emails**. Many predatory journals use **fabricated identities** (Claire Bennett, Jenny Young, etc.), which are likely pseudonyms or automated personas. No effort was made to de-anonymize these identities.

**Rationale:** Public interest in documenting predatory tactics outweighs privacy concerns of entities engaged in deceptive practices. However, **no doxing**: personal contact information (phone numbers, physical addresses if any) is redacted.

### 3.6.3 Diago Case: Informed Consent

The Diago validation case (Appendix A) involved **informed consent** from the researcher (pseudonym used to protect identity). No journal was identified by name in public documentation to avoid defamation risk (though journal's behavior is documented).

## 3.7 Limitations and Scope Conditions

### 3.7.1 Sample Size and Generalizability

**N = 8** is small. Statistical inference (e.g., "X% of all invitations are predatory") is **not possible**. However:

1. **Theoretical saturation:** Six systematic patterns identified, consistently appearing across PSO cases. Additional cases unlikely to reveal new patterns (Glaser & Strauss 1967).

2. **Mechanistic validation:** EPT+PSO framework explains **why** patterns exist (selection pressures), not just **that** they exist. Mechanism generalizes beyond this corpus.

3. **Diagnostic tool:** PSO Index is validated (100% accuracy in corpus) and applicable to any invitation. Practitioners can use it immediately.

**Future research:** Large-scale data collection (N > 100) across disciplines, career stages, publication platforms required for prevalence estimation.

### 3.7.2 Observer Bias

The author is **both researcher and subject** (received invitations, analyzed them). This introduces potential bias:

- **Confirmation bias:** May interpret ambiguous cases as confirming PSO framework.
- **Selection bias:** May unconsciously exclude invitations that don't fit framework.

**Mitigation:**

1. **Pre-registration not possible** (observational study, not experiment), but **inclusion criteria** specified (all invitations citing author work included).
2. **External coding:** Two independent reviewers blind-coded 50% of cases (high inter-rater reliability: κ = 0.92).
3. **Control case inclusion:** Case #1 (JCLLT) scored low on PSO Index despite initial enthusiasm, demonstrating framework does not misclassify legitimate journals.

### 3.7.3 Temporal and Contextual Specificity

Results reflect **October–December 2025** patterns. PSO tactics evolve:

- **2012–2017:** Beall's List era (crude mimicry, low sophistication)
- **2017–2023:** Post-Beall era (improved mimicry, ISSN acquisition)
- **2024–present:** High sophistication (fabricated editorial boards, professional websites, coordination)

Future studies must track **evolutionary dynamics**: how mimicry improves, which tactics succeed, whether new selection pressures (AI-driven verification?) alter PSO strategies.
