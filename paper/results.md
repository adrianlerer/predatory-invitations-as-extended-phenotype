## Dimension-by-Dimension Analysis

### Dimension 1: Disciplinary Mismatch

Six of seven parasitic invitations (85.7%) exhibited severe disciplinary mismatch (Table 2). These invitations cited legal evolution theory research but solicited submissions to journals in unrelated fields:

- **Case #2** (International Journal of Financial and Economic Studies): Legal theory paper invited to economics journal focused on financial markets and monetary policy
- **Case #3** (Journal of Statistics and Data Science): Legal theory paper invited to journal focused on statistical methodology and computational data analysis
- **Case #4** (International Journal of Journalism and Mass Communication): Legal theory paper invited to journalism journal focused on media studies and communication theory
- **Case #6** (Glint Journal of Economics and Management): Legal theory paper invited to economics journal focused on business management and organizational behavior
- **Case #7** (Academic Vision Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Visual Reality): Legal theory paper invited to computer science journal focused on AI/VR technology
- **Case #8** (Premier Journal of Geosciences): Legal theory paper invited to earth sciences journal focused on geology and environmental science

One parasitic invitation (Case #5, Journal of Healthcare and Emergency Care Research) exhibited moderate mismatch. Although the journal's stated scope was healthcare, the invitation text suggested openness to interdisciplinary work. However, no plausible connection between legal evolution theory and healthcare emergency research was articulated.

The legitimate control case (Case #1, Journal of Computational Law and Legal Technology) exhibited strong disciplinary match. The journal explicitly focuses on intersections of law, computation, and technology. Legal evolution theory, particularly work applying computational and evolutionary methods to legal analysis, falls squarely within the journal's stated scope. The invitation demonstrated substantive engagement with the paper's content, citing specific arguments and methodological innovations.

**Email attachment submission** was present in all parasitic cases (100%). Authors were instructed to submit manuscripts directly via email rather than through editorial management systems (OJS, ScholarOne, Manuscript Central). This mechanism eliminates the structured workflow that legitimate journals use to track submissions, assign reviewers, and manage revisions.

**Rapid review promises** appeared in 85.7% of parasitic cases. Claimed timelines ranged from "7-10 days" to "2-4 weeks." These timelines are implausible for genuine peer review. Typical legitimate journal review cycles span 2-6 months (Björk & Solomon, 2013), accounting for reviewer recruitment, multiple review rounds, and editorial deliberation.

**No formatting requirements** appeared in 57.1% of parasitic cases. These invitations stated "submit in any format" or "format will be adjusted by our team." Legitimate journals require adherence to specific citation styles, figure formatting, and manuscript structure to ensure consistency and facilitate review.

Case #5 (Journal of Healthcare and Emergency Care Research) exhibited the most aggressive friction reduction, including an acceptance guarantee: "We are confident your work will meet our publication standards and look forward to publishing it." This language implies acceptance is predetermined, contradicting the concept of peer review as quality filter.

The legitimate control case exhibited zero friction-reducing mechanisms. It directed authors to a standard OJS submission portal, specified formatting requirements, and provided realistic review timelines (4-6 months). No guarantees or shortcuts were offered.

These formulations are characteristic of mail-merge systems where variable fields (author name, paper title) are inserted into standardized templates.

**Simultaneous batches** occurred in 71.4% of parasitic cases. On October 15, 2024, the author received four invitations within a three-hour window (Cases #2, #3, #4, #6) from four different publishers requesting submissions to journals in completely unrelated fields. This simultaneity is inconsistent with individual editorial review but consistent with automated database scraping followed by batch email generation.

- "Following up on our previous invitation" (Case #5)
- "Per our earlier correspondence" (Case #7)

Neither case involved prior contact. The false follow-up creates illusion of ongoing relationship, exploiting the reciprocity norm while revealing automated system error (the "follow-up" logic triggered inappropriately).

The legitimate control case exhibited zero automation indicators. The invitation was personalized (used author's first name), referenced specific paper content ("your discussion of evolutionary game theory in legal context is particularly innovative"), and arrived independently rather than in a batch. The tone and content indicated individual human authorship.

## Pattern Stability Across Time and Publishers

### Temporal Stability

Invitations received in 2024 (n=4) exhibit statistically indistinguishable PSO scores from those received in 2025-2026 (n=4):

- Mean PSO score 2024: 0.89 (SD = 0.06)
- Mean PSO score 2025-2026: 0.88 (SD = 0.05)
- t-test: t(5) = 0.23, p = 0.83

This temporal stability suggests parasitic strategies are evolutionarily stable equilibria rather than transient tactics. Over an 18-month observation period, predatory publishers continued employing the same core strategies despite growing public awareness and educational interventions.

### Cross-Publisher Consistency

Seven parasitic invitations originated from seven nominally independent publishers (Academix, Explore Science, OASK, Premier Publishers, Glint Open Access, Academic Vision, Premier). Despite this apparent diversity, PSO patterns are remarkably consistent:

- **Coefficient of variation (PSO scores)**: 0.06 (very low dispersion)
- **Pattern correlation**: r > 0.85 for all pairwise publisher comparisons
- **Indicator overlap**: 78% of specific indicators appear in 5+ of 7 parasitic cases

This consistency in the absence of overt coordination supports the PSO framework: similar selection pressures produce convergent institutional evolution. Publishers independently discover that certain tactics (disciplinary indifference, emotional manipulation, friction reduction, economic opacity, automation) enhance manuscript acquisition. These tactics are selected for and replicate, creating recognizable patterns without requiring communication among publishers.

The pattern mirrors biological examples of convergent evolution: unrelated species facing similar environmental pressures independently evolve similar adaptations (e.g., streamlined body forms in sharks, dolphins, and ichthyosaurs). Here, unrelated publishers facing similar market pressures independently evolve similar parasitic strategies.

An unexpected empirical finding emerged during detailed analysis: multiple predatory invitations cited ISSN numbers with a common prefix pattern (Table 11).

Three parasitic journals (42.9% of parasitic cases) share the **3069-XXXX** pattern, despite originating from different publishers (Explore Science, Glint Open Access, Academic Vision). 

ISSN numbers are assigned by national ISSN agencies according to publishers' registration locations. The 3069 prefix was allocated to publishers registering in specific jurisdictions in 2020-2023. The clustering suggests:

1. **Shared infrastructure**: These "independent" publishers may actually share back-end registration, web hosting, or editorial systems.
2. **Temporal clustering**: All three registered during the same period (2020-2023), consistent with rapid emergence of parasitic publishing during COVID-19 era.
3. **Jurisdictional concentration**: The publishers may be registering through the same national ISSN center, indicating geographic concentration of parasitic operations.

This pattern was not anticipated by the theoretical framework but provides additional evidence for viewing predatory publishing as coherent institutional phenomenon rather than isolated fraud. The publishers may be legally distinct entities but functionally part of a shared ecosystem.

## Hypothesis Testing

The theoretical framework generated four testable predictions:

**H1: Pattern Consistency Across Independent Publishers**  
*Prediction*: Parasitic invitations from nominally independent publishers will exhibit similar characteristics because they face similar selection pressures.  
*Result*: **SUPPORTED**. PSO score CV = 0.06; pattern correlation r > 0.85; 78% indicator overlap.

**H2: Divergence from Legitimate Practice**  
*Prediction*: Parasitic invitations will systematically differ from legitimate editorial outreach across multiple dimensions simultaneously.  
*Result*: **SUPPORTED**. All six dimensions show strong separation; 100% classification accuracy.

**H3: Mimetic Sophistication**  
*Prediction*: Some parasitic publishers will exhibit high mimetic legitimacy (sophisticated surface-level mimicry) while maintaining other parasitic characteristics.  
*Result*: **SUPPORTED**. Case #8 matches legitimate case on mimetic legitimacy (8/8 indicators) but scores 0.89 on PSO Index due to disciplinary mismatch, emotional manipulation, and automation indicators.

**H4: Automation Enabling Scale**  
*Prediction*: Parasitic invitations will exhibit evidence of automated generation, enabling high-volume outreach.  
*Result*: **SUPPORTED**. 85.7% of parasitic cases show template language; 71.4% arrive in simultaneous batches; 100% show exact title scraping without abstract engagement.

All four hypotheses are supported by the empirical data. No predictions were disconfirmed.

## Limitations and Boundary Cases

### Case #5 Anomaly

Case #5 (Journal of Healthcare and Emergency Care Research) exhibits unusual characteristics:

- **Economic transparency**: Only parasitic case to disclose APC ($198 USD) in initial invitation
- **Lower PSO score**: 0.78, lowest among parasitic cases but still above 0.60 threshold
- **Mixed signals**: Some friction reduction (rapid review, acceptance guarantee) but less automation evidence

This case suggests a "hybrid" form: parasitic in disciplinary indifference and quality standards but more transparent in economic terms. It may represent either:

1. **Evolutionary experimentation**: A publisher testing whether transparency increases submission rates by reducing suspicion.
2. **Honest low-quality outlet**: A journal that genuinely lacks rigorous standards but does not actively deceive about fees.
3. **Measurement error**: The case may be less parasitic than coded, reflecting limitations of the six-dimension framework.

Sensitivity analysis excluding Case #5 yields mean PSO score of 0.89 (SD = 0.04) for remaining parasitic cases, slightly increasing separation from the legitimate case. Classification accuracy remains 100%.

### Small Sample and Generalizability

The corpus comprises only eight invitations from one researcher. This limits generalizability:

- **Field specificity**: Legal theory and institutional economics may attract different predatory publishers than medicine or engineering.
- **Career stage**: An established mid-career scholar may receive different invitations than early-career or senior researchers.
- **Geographic targeting**: An Argentina-based author may be targeted differently than US or European authors.

However, several factors support broader generalizability:

1. **Literature consistency**: Patterns observed here match those reported in medical publishing (Cobey et al., 2018; Grudniewicz et al., 2019), engineering (Xia et al., 2015), and humanities (Butler, 2013).
2. **Diago validation**: The Brazilian case (different field, career stage, country) exhibits identical patterns.
3. **Theoretical derivation**: Patterns are predicted by EPT/PSO framework, not inductively discovered from data, increasing external validity.

### Legitimate Journal Variation

Not all legitimate journals match the high standards of Case #1 (JCLLT). Some legitimate journals exhibit characteristics that partially overlap with parasitic patterns:

- **Rapid review**: Some legitimate journals (e.g., PLOS ONE) offer fast-track review for a fee.
- **Broad scope**: Mega-journals (e.g., Scientific Reports) accept work across disciplines.
- **Author solicitation**: Some legitimate journals actively recruit submissions through mass email.

These overlaps create boundary cases where classification is ambiguous. The PSO Index threshold (0.20) is deliberately conservative to minimize false positives (legitimate journals misclassified as parasitic). A more aggressive threshold (e.g., 0.40) might capture additional parasitic cases but would risk false positives.

Future work should expand the corpus to include more legitimate journals with varying characteristics, enabling refinement of thresholds and weights.

## Summary of Key Findings

1. **Perfect Classification**: The PSO Index achieves 100% classification accuracy (8/8) in distinguishing parasitic from legitimate invitations.

2. **Systematic Patterns**: Seven parasitic invitations exhibit remarkably consistent characteristics across six dimensions, despite originating from nominally independent publishers.

3. **Disciplinary Indifference**: 85.7% of parasitic invitations exhibit severe disciplinary mismatch, the strongest single discriminatory feature.

4. **Sophisticated Mimicry**: Parasitic publishers invest in legitimacy signals (ISSN, editorial boards, professional presentation) while maintaining parasitic strategies.

5. **Cognitive Exploitation**: All parasitic invitations employ multiple emotional manipulation tactics (mean 3.7/5) targeting authority bias, scarcity bias, and reciprocity norms.

6. **Automation at Scale**: 85.7% of parasitic invitations show template language and batch generation, enabling high-volume outreach at minimal cost.

7. **Economic Opacity**: 85.7% of parasitic invitations fail to disclose APCs upfront, exploiting sunk cost bias.

8. **Temporal Stability**: Patterns remain consistent over 18 months, suggesting evolutionary stability rather than transient tactics.

9. **Cross-Publisher Convergence**: High pattern consistency (r > 0.85) across seven independent publishers supports spontaneous order interpretation over conspiracy theory.

10. **ISSN Clustering**: 42.9% of parasitic journals share the 3069-XXXX ISSN pattern, suggesting shared infrastructure or jurisdictional concentration.

These findings provide strong empirical support for the Extended Phenotype Theory / Parasitic Spontaneous Order framework. Predatory journal invitations are not random fraud but rather systematic institutional adaptations selected for their effectiveness in exploiting structural vulnerabilities and cognitive biases in the academic publishing ecosystem.
