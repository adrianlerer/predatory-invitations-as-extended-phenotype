# 2. Theoretical Framework: Extended Phenotype Theory and Parasitic Spontaneous Order

## 2.1 Foundations: Replicator Dynamics and Selection Pressures

### 2.1.1 The Gene's-Eye View Applied to Institutions

Dawkins' *The Selfish Gene* (1976) revolutionized evolutionary biology by shifting analytical focus from organisms to **replicators**—entities that make copies of themselves and compete for representation in future generations. Genes are biological replicators; memes (ideas, practices, institutional forms) are cultural replicators. Natural selection operates on differential replication: variants that produce more surviving copies proliferate, regardless of organism welfare.

The key insight: **What replicates is what we observe.** Organisms are "vehicles" constructed by genes to facilitate replication. The question is not "Why does this organism behave this way?" but "What gene (or meme) is being replicated by this behavior?"

Applied to academic publishing:

- **Replicator:** The "published paper" token—a cultural unit that signals productivity, expertise, and institutional value. It replicates when recognized by hiring committees, promotion panels, funding agencies, and citation indices.

- **Vehicle:** The individual researcher, whose career depends on accumulating these tokens.

- **Extended phenotype:** The journal system—the institutional infrastructure that processes, validates, and certifies these tokens.

In a **functional system**, the extended phenotype (journal) aligns with epistemic goals: peer review filters low-quality work, editorial boards ensure field relevance, and publication signals genuine contribution. The replicator (paper token) correlates with actual knowledge production.

In a **parasitic system**, the extended phenotype diverges: journals replicate the **signals** of validation (ISSN, DOI, "peer review" claims) while abandoning the **function** (quality filtering). The replicator proliferates through **credential inflation**, not epistemic contribution.

### 2.1.2 Selection Pressures in Metric-Driven Academia

Modern academic systems impose three interlocking selection pressures:

**Pressure 1: Publish or Perish**  
Hiring, tenure, promotion, and funding depend on quantifiable publication metrics. Researchers face evolutionary pressure to maximize publication count, not just quality. This creates demand for **any** venue that provides the "published paper" token.

**Pressure 2: Metric Substitution**  
Institutions cannot directly observe research quality (epistemic contribution is costly to evaluate), so they substitute observable proxies: publication count, journal impact factor, citation metrics. This creates incentives to game metrics rather than produce knowledge.

**Pressure 3: Verification Costs**  
Distinguishing legitimate from predatory journals requires time, expertise, and access to indexing databases. Researchers under time pressure face high information costs. This creates opportunities for **mimicry**: entities that replicate legitimacy signals at low cost can exploit verification failures.

These pressures create a **niche** for PSO: journals that minimize validation costs (no genuine peer review) while maximizing legitimacy signals (ISSN acquisition, professional websites, fabricated editorial boards). Such journals outcompete both:

- **High-quality journals:** Costly peer review, high rejection rates, long timelines.
- **Obvious scams:** No legitimacy signals, zero acceptance by discerning authors.

PSO occupies the **intermediate strategy:** enough mimicry to pass superficial inspection, enough volume to sustain operations despite low per-author revenue.

## 2.2 Extended Phenotype Theory: Mechanistic Application

### 2.2.1 Phenotypic Expression: Mimicry Without Function

Dawkins (1982) emphasizes that extended phenotypes can manipulate other organisms' behavior to serve the replicator's interests, not the manipulated organism's. Classic example: the lancet fluke (*Dicrocoelium dendriticum*) infects ants and alters their behavior, causing them to climb grass blades and remain exposed, increasing the probability of being eaten by grazing mammals (the fluke's definitive host). The ant's behavior is the **extended phenotype** of fluke genes.

Key features of parasitic extended phenotypes:

1. **Mimicry of adaptive behavior:** The infected ant climbs as if seeking optimal feeding, but the behavior serves the parasite, not the ant.

2. **Functional divergence:** The behavior looks normal (climbing) but achieves parasitic ends (transmission to mammal host).

3. **Stability through host ignorance:** Ants do not "detect" the manipulation; if they did, selection would favor resistance.

Applied to predatory journals:

| **Biological Parasitism** | **Institutional Parasitism (PSO)** |
|---------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Parasite manipulates host behavior | Journal mimics validation signals |
| Behavior looks adaptive to host | Journal looks legitimate to author |
| Serves parasite's reproduction | Serves credential extraction |
| Host ignorance required | Author verification costs high |
| Stability through mimicry | Stability through ISSN/DOI/peer review claims |

Predatory journals are **extended phenotypes** of dysfunctional incentive structures. They replicate because they exploit authors' need for publication tokens under high verification costs. Content quality is irrelevant because the function is credential vending, not knowledge validation.

### 2.2.2 Cuckoo Strategy: Resource Extraction via Mimicry

The European cuckoo (*Cuculus canorus*) lays eggs in other birds' nests. Cuckoo eggs mimic host eggs (size, color, pattern), exploiting host parental instincts. After hatching, cuckoo chicks eject host eggs and monopolize parental provisioning. Host birds provision cuckoo chicks as if they were their own offspring—an extended phenotype of cuckoo genes expressed through host behavior.

Critical features:

1. **Mimicry fidelity:** Cuckoo eggs must sufficiently resemble host eggs to avoid rejection. Selection pressure on hosts (favor rejection of foreign eggs) drives selection pressure on cuckoos (improve mimicry).

2. **Arms race:** Host detection improves → cuckoo mimicry improves → host detection improves. This is a **Red Queen dynamic**: both sides evolve, but neither achieves permanent advantage.

3. **Functional divergence:** Host behavior (provisioning chick) serves cuckoo reproduction, not host reproduction.

Applied to predatory publishing:

| **Cuckoo Strategy** | **PSO Journal Strategy** |
|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Egg mimics host eggs | Journal mimics legitimacy signals |
| Exploits parental instinct | Exploits publication pressure |
| Chick monopolizes provisioning | Journal extracts APCs without validation |
| Host detection drives mimicry refinement | Blacklists drive better mimicry (better websites, ISSN) |
| Stable despite detection (arms race) | Stable despite exposure (new journals replace blacklisted ones) |

The **arms race** explains why predatory publishing persists despite Beall's List, institutional warnings, and widespread exposure: as detection improves, mimicry improves. PSO entities invest in better websites, acquire ISSN numbers, fabricate editorial boards with real researcher names (without consent), and claim indexing in legitimate databases. This is not fraud becoming more sophisticated—it is **selection pressure** favoring better mimics.

### 2.2.3 Content Irrelevance: The Functional Test

If predatory journals were simply **low-quality** publishers (inadequate peer review, weak editorial standards), content would still matter: truly nonsensical papers would be rejected. But if predatory journals function as **credential vendors**, content is irrelevant except insofar as it provides plausible deniability ("we published something").

The **Diago case** (Appendix A) provides the critical test:

- **Hypothesis 1 (low-quality model):** Predatory journals accept weak but coherent papers. Absurd papers are rejected.
- **Hypothesis 2 (PSO model):** Predatory journals accept any paper (coherent or absurd) conditional on APC payment.

**Result:** Diago's intentionally absurd paper (containing mathematical errors, nonsensical claims, and internally contradictory arguments) was accepted for publication with minor formatting requests. No substantive review occurred.

**Interpretation:** This confirms EPT Principle 3 (functional divergence). The journal's **claimed function** is quality validation; its **actual function** is credential vending. Content is irrelevant.

## 2.3 Spontaneous Order Theory: Emergence Without Conspiracy

### 2.3.1 Hayek's Core Insight: Order Without Design

Hayek (1973, 1988) distinguished two types of order:

1. **Taxis (designed order):** Created by deliberate human planning (a factory layout, a software architecture).
2. **Kosmos (spontaneous order):** Emerges from decentralized actions following general rules, without central coordination (market prices, common law, language evolution).

Key features of spontaneous order:

- **No mastermind:** No single actor designs the outcome.
- **Local knowledge:** Participants act on dispersed, context-specific information.
- **Emergent structure:** Patterns arise from interactions, not blueprints.
- **Stability:** Order persists because it solves coordination problems or exploits institutional opportunities.

Hayek emphasized **beneficial spontaneous orders** (markets, common law) but acknowledged **parasitic spontaneous orders**: emergent structures that exploit system vulnerabilities without contributing proportional value. Regulatory capture, rent-seeking coalitions, and corruption networks exemplify PSO.

### 2.3.2 Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO): Operational Definition

**Definition:** A parasitic spontaneous order is an emergent institutional configuration that:

1. **Arises without central coordination** (no conspiracy, no mastermind)
2. **Replicates institutional signals** of legitimate entities (ISSN, DOI, peer review claims)
3. **Extracts resources** from participants (author fees, credential inflation)
4. **Provides minimal epistemic contribution** (no genuine quality validation)
5. **Persists through selection pressures** (outcompetes both high-quality and obviously fraudulent entities)

Critical distinction from fraud:

| **Fraud** | **PSO** |
|-----------|---------|
| Intentional deception | Emergent exploitation |
| Violates law | Exploits legal incentives |
| Centrally coordinated | Decentralized emergence |
| Detectable → eliminated | Detectable → mimicry improves |
| Individual responsibility | Systemic dysfunction |

PSO entities may operate **legally**: they obtain ISSN numbers through legitimate channels, provide DOI registration (even if through less reputable agencies like Crossref alternatives), and claim "peer review" (a legally undefined term). They exploit **dysfunctional incentives**, not legal violations.

### 2.3.3 Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)

Maynard Smith (1982) introduced the concept of **evolutionarily stable strategy**: a behavioral strategy that, if adopted by most members of a population, cannot be invaded by any alternative strategy. An ESS is a Nash equilibrium **plus** stability against mutations (rare alternative strategies).

Applied to predatory publishing:

**Population:** Academic journals (legitimate + PSO)

**Strategies:**
1. **High quality (HQ):** Rigorous peer review, high rejection rate, long timelines, high credibility.
2. **PSO:** Mimicry of HQ signals, minimal review, rapid acceptance, low credibility among discerning authors.
3. **Obvious scam (OS):** No legitimacy signals, immediate rejection by all discerning authors.

**Payoff matrix** (simplified):

- **HQ journals:** Attract high-quality submissions, build reputation, but incur high costs (peer review infrastructure, editorial labor, low acceptance rate means low revenue if dependent on APCs).

- **PSO journals:** Attract desperate/naive authors, extract APCs, incur low costs (no genuine review), sustain operations through volume strategy.

- **OS journals:** Attract zero submissions from anyone, collapse.

**Key insight:** PSO is stable **conditional on dysfunctional author incentives**. If authors face "publish or perish" pressure, high verification costs, and institutional acceptance of publication counts (regardless of venue quality), PSO exploits this niche. Eliminating individual PSO journals does not eliminate the niche—new journals emerge to fill it.

**Why PSO persists despite exposure:**

1. **Selection niche remains:** Author incentives unchanged.
2. **Mimicry improves:** Exposed PSO entities are replaced by better mimics.
3. **Verification costs remain high:** Authors lack time/expertise to verify each invitation.
4. **Institutions lag:** Hiring/promotion committees update slowly.

This is **not** a market failure that transparency solves. It is a **systemic equilibrium** that requires changing underlying incentives.

## 2.4 Synthesis: EPT + Spontaneous Order = PSO Framework

### 2.4.1 The Integrated Model

| **Component** | **EPT Mechanism** | **Spontaneous Order Mechanism** | **Predatory Publishing Example** |
|---------------|-------------------|---------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Replicator** | Gene / Meme | Institutional practice | "Published paper" token |
| **Vehicle** | Organism | Individual agent | Researcher seeking promotion |
| **Phenotype** | Extended structure | Emergent rule system | Journal infrastructure (ISSN, DOI, website) |
| **Selection pressure** | Differential replication | Competitive advantage | Publish-or-perish + metric substitution |

| **Arms race** | Host detection vs. parasite mimicry | Regulation vs. evasion | Blacklists vs. improved mimicry |

### 2.4.2 Three Falsifiable Predictions

The EPT+PSO framework generates three testable predictions:

**Prediction 1: Mimetic Fidelity Increases with Exposure**  
*Mechanism:* As detection efforts improve (blacklists, institutional warnings), PSO entities face selection pressure to improve mimicry. Low-fidelity mimics are detected and avoided; high-fidelity mimics survive.

*Test:* Longitudinal analysis should show that newer PSO journals exhibit better mimicry (professional websites, ISSN acquisition, fabricated editorial boards) than older exposed ones.

*Evidence (preliminary):* Cases in this corpus (2025) exhibit higher mimicry fidelity than Beall's List examples (2012–2017). Case #6 and #7 use ISSN numbers in the 3069-XXXX range, suggesting coordinated acquisition; websites use professional templates; sender identities use plausible Western names (Claire Bennett, Jenny Young).

**Prediction 2: Content Quality Is Irrelevant to Acceptance**  
*Mechanism:* PSO journals function as credential vendors, not epistemic validators. Manuscript content does not affect acceptance decisions (conditional on APC payment).

*Test:* Submit intentionally absurd papers to suspected PSO journals. If PSO, papers are accepted; if low-quality legitimate journals, papers are rejected.

*Evidence:* Diago case (Appendix A) confirms. Absurd paper accepted with formatting requests only, no substantive review.

**Prediction 3: Disciplinary Mismatch Is Stable**  
*Mechanism:* PSO journals scrape publications automatically from databases (SSRN, ResearchGate, Google Scholar) and send invitations indiscriminately. They optimize for volume, not precision. Disciplinary mismatch (law paper to geoscience journal) persists because a small percentage of desperate authors accept anyway.

*Test:* Corpus analysis should reveal systematic disciplinary mismatches.

*Evidence:* This corpus: 6/7 PSO cases (85.7%) exhibit dramatic mismatch (law paper to economics, statistics, journalism, geosciences, AI/VR). Only 1/7 (Case #1, JCLLT) exhibits perfect alignment.

### 2.4.3 Distinguishing PSO from Alternative Explanations

**Alternative 1: Simple Fraud**  
*Claim:* Predatory journals are criminal enterprises intentionally deceiving authors.

*Problems:*
1. **Legality:** PSO journals often operate legally (obtain ISSN, provide DOI, claim undefined "peer review").
2. **Decentralization:** No mastermind; thousands of independent entities emerge.
3. **Stability:** Fraud exposed should collapse; PSO exposed adapts.

*Verdict:* Fraud is **insufficient**. PSO includes fraud but also legal exploitation of dysfunctional incentives.

**Alternative 2: Principal-Agent Problem**  
*Claim:* Authors (agents) exploit institutions (principals) by publishing in low-quality venues.

*Problems:*
1. **Wrong direction:** PSO journals exploit authors, not vice versa (authors pay APCs, inflate CVs with low-value credentials).
2. **Triadic structure:** Author-journal-institution, not bilateral.
3. **Emergence:** Principal-agent requires contract; PSO emerges without agreement.

*Verdict:* Principal-agent captures **part** of the dynamic (author incentives) but not emergence, mimicry, or stability.

**Alternative 3: Information Asymmetry**  
*Claim:* Authors lack information about journal quality; transparency solves the problem.

*Problems:*
1. **Persistence:** Extensive transparency efforts (Beall's List, Think.Check.Submit, institutional guides) have not reduced PSO prevalence.
2. **Mimicry response:** PSO entities adapt to transparency by improving mimicry, not withdrawing.
3. **Selection niche:** Even informed authors may submit to PSO journals under extreme publication pressure.

*Verdict:* Information asymmetry is **necessary but insufficient**. PSO persists because incentives, not just information, are dysfunctional.

**Alternative 4: Regulatory Capture**  
*Claim:* Predatory publishers "capture" academic governance.

*Problems:*
1. **No regulator:** Academic publishing is decentralized; no single regulator to capture.
2. **No lobbying:** PSO journals do not lobby for favorable rules; they exploit existing rules.
3. **Spontaneous:** Regulatory capture is deliberate; PSO is emergent.

*Verdict:* Wrong mechanism. PSO is **evolutionary**, not **political**.

## 2.5 Policy Implications: Incentive Modification, Not Blacklisting

The EPT+PSO framework implies that **symptom-focused interventions fail**:

**Ineffective Approach 1: Blacklists (Beall's List)**  
*Problem:* Whac-a-Mole. New PSO journals replace blacklisted ones faster than lists update. Arms race, not solution.

**Ineffective Approach 2: Author Education**  
*Problem:* High verification costs remain. Desperate authors submit anyway. Information is necessary but insufficient.

**Ineffective Approach 3: Legal Action**  
*Problem:* Most PSO journals operate legally. Even illegal ones (using researcher names without consent) are jurisdictionally difficult to prosecute (offshore entities).

**Effective Approach: Modify Selection Environment**  
*Solution:* Change what institutions reward.

1. **Hiring/promotion committees:** Evaluate research impact (citations, real-world application, methodological contribution), not publication count. Manual review required.

2. **Funding agencies:** Require narrative justification of research significance, not CV length. De-emphasize metrics.

3. **Universities:** Adopt "San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment" (DORA) principles—ban use of journal impact factors in evaluation.

4. **Preprint culture:** Normalize preprint servers (SSRN, arXiv) as primary dissemination; journals become post-hoc certification, not gatekeeping.

5. **Open peer review:** Make reviews public, credit reviewers. Reduces anonymity that enables low-quality review.

**Key insight:** PSO persists because underlying incentives reward publication volume. Eliminating PSO requires eliminating the niche, not the occupants.

## 2.6 Generalization: Beyond Academic Publishing

The EPT+PSO framework generalizes to any domain where:

1. **Credential tokens** substitute for direct quality evaluation
2. **Mimicry costs** are lower than genuine production costs
3. **Verification costs** are high for participants
4. **Selection pressures** reward volume over quality

Examples:

- **Citation rings:** Authors collude to inflate citation counts without genuine intellectual engagement.
- **Conference mills:** Pay-to-present events provide "conference presentation" credential without peer review.
- **Ranking manipulation:** Journals game impact factors through coercive citation, self-citation cartels.
- **Diploma mills:** Degree-granting institutions provide credentials without education.
- **Certification agencies:** Third-party certifiers (ISO, industry standards) provide compliance credentials without genuine auditing.
