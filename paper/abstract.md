# Abstract

**Title:** Spontaneous Parasitic Order: Predatory Publishing as Extended Phenotype

**Author:** Ignacio Adrián Lerer

---

## Abstract (250 words)

Predatory academic journals are commonly understood as isolated instances of fraud or ethical failure. This paper proposes an alternative framework: predatory publishing represents **Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO)**—an emergent institutional configuration that evolves through selection pressures inherent in metric-driven academic systems. Drawing on Richard Dawkins' Extended Phenotype Theory and F.A. Hayek's concept of spontaneous order, I argue that predatory journals function as extended phenotypes of dysfunctional incentive structures, replicating institutional signals (ISSN, DOI, "peer review") while extracting resources without proportional epistemic contribution.

Through qualitative analysis of eight invitation emails (seven predatory, one legitimate control), I document six systematic patterns: (1) disciplinary mismatch exploitation, (2) mimetic legitimacy signaling, (3) emotional manipulation mechanisms, (4) friction minimization strategies, (5) automation indicators, and (6) financial extraction approaches. The **PSO Index** (0-5 scale) successfully distinguishes predatory from legitimate journals with 100% accuracy in this corpus.

The Extended Phenotype Theory framework explains three otherwise puzzling features: (1) stability despite exposure (PSO persists because underlying selection pressures remain), (2) diversification of tactics (different PSO variants exploit different author vulnerabilities), and (3) content irrelevance (as validated by the Diago experiment, where absurd papers were accepted). 

Rather than isolated fraud, predatory publishing represents an **evolutionarily stable strategy** within credential-dependent systems. Policy implications focus on modifying selection environments (changing what institutions reward) rather than blacklist-based approaches. The framework generalizes to other domains exhibiting similar parasitic dynamics: citation rings, conference mills, and ranking manipulation.

**Keywords:** predatory publishing, extended phenotype theory, spontaneous order, institutional parasitism, academic integrity, evolutionary game theory

## Significance Statement (150 words)

This research reframes predatory academic publishing from individual fraud to systemic phenomenon, applying evolutionary biology's Extended Phenotype Theory to institutional analysis. The key insight: predatory journals are not anomalies but predictable outcomes of selection pressures in metric-driven academic systems. By treating "published paper" as a cultural replicator and journals as extended phenotypes, the framework explains why predatory publishing persists despite exposure, why it diversifies, and why content quality is irrelevant to its function.

Methodologically, the paper develops a quantitative **Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO) Index** validated across eight real-world cases, providing practitioners with diagnostic tools. Theoretically, it bridges evolutionary biology, institutional economics, and legal theory, offering a general framework applicable beyond academic publishing. Practically, it shifts policy focus from reactive blacklisting to proactive incentive realignment, addressing root causes rather than symptoms.
