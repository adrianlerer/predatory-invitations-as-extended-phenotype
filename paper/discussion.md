## Theoretical Implications

### Extended Phenotype Theory Beyond Biology

The empirical findings demonstrate that Extended Phenotype Theory (EPT), originally formulated to explain evolutionary dynamics in biological systems, successfully predicts institutional patterns in academic publishing. This extension is not merely metaphorical. The formal structure of EPT applies wherever three conditions hold:

1. **Replicators exist**: Institutional forms (predatory publishing models) replicate through imitation, resource flows, and information transmission.

2. **Variation exists**: Different publishers employ different tactics (email scraping vs. targeted outreach; rapid review vs. acceptance guarantees; transparent pricing vs. opacity).

3. **Selection operates**: Tactics that attract more submissions, evade detection, and minimize costs are selected for; ineffective tactics are selected against.

These conditions manifestly obtain in academic publishing. The parasitic invitation patterns documented in this study are not designed by any central authority but emerge through decentralized evolutionary dynamics. Publishers that accidentally discovered effective tactics (e.g., disciplinary indifference expands target pool; emotional manipulation increases response rates; automation reduces costs) replicated more successfully than competitors. Over time, the institutional population converged on a recognizable syndrome of characteristics.

The theoretical advance lies in recognizing that **the unit of selection is not the publisher (the "organism") but the institutional form itself** (the "gene"). Individual publishers may fail, be shut down, or change practices, but the parasitic institutional pattern persists because it replicates through new entrants. This is precisely analogous to biological evolution: individual organisms die, but genes persist by copying themselves into new bodies.

The extended phenotype concept reveals why downstream effects (solicitation emails, mimetic legitimacy signals, friction reduction mechanisms) matter evolutionarily. These effects are not epiphenomena or mere symptoms of parasitic intent; they are the *mechanism* by which the institutional form replicates. An invitation email that successfully induces manuscript submission propagates the parasitic form. Mimetic legitimacy signals that reduce detection increase institutional fitness. Friction reduction that lowers submission barriers enhances replication. Each extended phenotypic effect contributes to the form's evolutionary success.

This perspective transforms policy analysis. If predatory publishing were simple fraud (malicious actors deceiving victims), the solution would be prosecution and education. But if predatory publishing is an extended phenotype shaped by institutional selection, then effective interventions must alter the selection pressures that maintain the parasitic equilibrium.

### Parasitic Spontaneous Order as General Framework

The concept of Parasitic Spontaneous Order (PSO) extends beyond academic publishing to characterize a broad class of institutional phenomena. PSO describes coordination patterns that:

- Emerge without central planning (spontaneous)
- Exhibit self-reinforcing dynamics (stable equilibria)
- Extract value asymmetrically (parasitic)
- Replicate through extended phenotypic effects (evolutionarily fit)
- Mimic beneficial institutions (deceptive)
- Resist elimination despite recognition (evolutionarily stable)

This definition applies to multiple domains documented in existing literature:

**Financial predation**: Payday lending, rent-to-own contracts, and predatory mortgage products coordinate around extracting value from individuals with limited financial literacy or constrained choices (Bar-Gill, 2012). These markets exhibit spontaneous order (no central coordination), mimicry of legitimate financial services (professional presentation, regulatory compliance), and evolutionary stability (continuous re-emergence despite regulatory crackdowns).

**Regulatory capture**: Industries spontaneously coordinate to influence regulatory processes, producing rules that benefit regulated firms at public expense (Stigler, 1971; Laffont & Tirole, 1991). The pattern emerges without conspiracy (individual firms lobby independently) yet exhibits systematic characteristics (revolving door employment, concentrated benefits/diffuse costs, technical complexity that obscures extraction).

**Credential inflation**: Educational institutions, professional associations, and employers coordinate around requiring ever-higher credentials for jobs that do not genuinely need them, extracting tuition fees while providing minimal skill enhancement (Caplan, 2018). This pattern spontaneously intensifies (no one plans credential inflation) yet stably persists (unilateral deviation is individually costly).

**Citation rings**: Researchers coordinate to mutually cite each other's work, inflating bibliometric indicators without substantive intellectual contribution (Fister et al., 2016). The pattern emerges spontaneously (individuals discover mutual benefit), mimics legitimate citation (appears as scholarly engagement), and resists elimination (new rings form as old ones are detected).

**Astroturfing**: Fake grassroots movements coordinate to simulate public opinion, extracting political influence without genuine popular support (Lyon & Maxwell, 2004). The institutional form replicates across issues and jurisdictions, exhibits recognizable characteristics (emotional appeals, manufactured urgency, mimetic legitimacy), and adapts to detection mechanisms.

The common structural features across these domains suggest PSO is a general institutional pathology, not specific to academic publishing. Wherever the following conditions obtain, PSO is likely to emerge:

1. **Asymmetric information**: One party has knowledge the other lacks.
2. **Coordination failure**: No mechanism exists to coordinate collective rejection.
3. **Metric distortion**: Proxy measures replace direct evaluation of value.
4. **Mimicry feasibility**: Parasitic forms can superficially resemble beneficial institutions.
5. **Structural embeddedness**: The pattern exploits deep features (cognitive biases, organizational routines) resistant to reform.

Understanding PSO as general phenomenon enables cross-domain learning. Interventions successful in one domain may transfer to others. Failures in one domain illuminate why similar interventions fail elsewhere. The theoretical framework provides unified conceptual vocabulary for analyzing disparate empirical cases.

### Game-Theoretic Formalization

The evolutionary stability of PSO can be formalized using evolutionary game theory. Consider a population of researchers choosing between three publication strategies:

- **Elite (E)**: Submit only to top-tier journals (high rejection, long timelines, high prestige)
- **Mainstream (M)**: Submit to mid-tier legitimate journals (moderate acceptance, moderate timelines, moderate prestige)
- **Parasitic (P)**: Submit to predatory journals (guaranteed acceptance, immediate publication, zero prestige)

And a population of publishers choosing between two business models:

- **Legitimate (L)**: Rigorous peer review, disciplinary focus, transparent pricing, selective acceptance
- **Parasitic (Pa)**: Minimal review, disciplinary indifference, opaque pricing, universal acceptance

The system has four possible equilibria:

**Equilibrium 1: (E + M, L)** - Researchers use elite and mainstream strategies; only legitimate publishers survive. This is the socially optimal outcome but requires:
- Institutional incentives reward quality over quantity
- Researchers have sufficient time to navigate rejection and revision
- Career progression does not depend on publication counts

**Equilibrium 2: (P, Pa)** - Researchers use parasitic strategy; only parasitic publishers survive. This is the current observed equilibrium in many contexts, maintained by:
- Institutional incentives reward publication counts over quality
- Time pressure (tenure clocks, visa renewals) makes acceptance speed valuable
- Reputational costs of predatory submission are low (many others do it)

**Equilibrium 3: Mixed (E + M + P, L + Pa)** - Both researcher strategies and both publisher types coexist. This may be stable if:
- Different researcher populations face different incentive structures
- Some publishers can profitably serve niche markets (rapid turnaround, guaranteed acceptance)
- Reputational sorting partially segments the market

**Equilibrium 4: Unstable Transition** - Attempts to move from Equilibrium 2 to Equilibrium 1 through partial interventions (blacklists, education) face coordination problems. Individual researchers who unilaterally avoid predatory venues sacrifice publication speed without changing the system. Individual publishers who adopt rigorous standards lose market share to parasitic competitors. The system reverts to Equilibrium 2 unless coordinated interventions change the payoff structure simultaneously for all actors.

The formal analysis reveals why standard interventions fail:

- **Education** increases awareness but does not change payoffs. Informed researchers still submit to predatory venues when institutional incentives demand publication counts.

- **Blacklisting** targets individual publishers but does not change incentives. New parasitic publishers immediately enter to serve the demand.

- **Whitelisting** creates mimicry pressure. Parasitic publishers adopt surface features of legitimate venues (ISSN registration, editorial boards, peer review claims) to game certification criteria.

- **Voluntary compliance** faces free-rider problems. Universities that unilaterally adopt quality-over-quantity tenure standards risk losing faculty recruitment battles to competitors still using count-based metrics.

Effective intervention requires **coordinated equilibrium shift**: changing payoff structures simultaneously across multiple institutional layers (universities, funding agencies, visa authorities, journal certification systems). This is politically and logistically difficult but theoretically necessary to escape the parasitic equilibrium.

## Addressing Theoretical Objections

The PSO framework faces ten potential objections. This section stress-tests the theory by addressing each in detail.

### Objection 1: "Some Invitations May Reflect Genuine Editorial Interest"

**Claim**: Disciplinary mismatch might reflect genuine interdisciplinary interest rather than predatory automation. A journal on statistics might legitimately want a paper on legal theory if it employs statistical methods.

**Response**: This objection fails for three reasons:

First, **content analysis refutes interdisciplinary interpretation**. The cited paper (Lerer, 2024a) uses game theory and evolutionary biology but contains no statistical methodology beyond basic logic. A statistics journal with genuine interest would cite the paper's methodological contribution, not merely its title. None of the parasitic invitations demonstrated substantive engagement with content.

Second, **pattern consistency refutes good-faith error**. A single disciplinary mismatch might reflect misjudgment. Seven simultaneous mismatches from independent publishers indicate systematic pattern, not coincidental mistakes.

Third, **legitimate interdisciplinary journals exhibit different characteristics**. Journals genuinely pursuing interdisciplinary scholarship (e.g., PLOS ONE, Science Advances) employ rigorous peer review, selective acceptance, and substantive editorial engagement. They do not combine disciplinary indifference with emotional manipulation, friction reduction, and economic opacity. The syndrome of characteristics jointly discriminates parasitic from legitimate interdisciplinary outreach.

### Objection 2: "Market Inefficiency, Not Parasitism"

**Claim**: Predatory publishing reflects market inefficiency (information asymmetry, search costs) rather than parasitism. It is a "lemons problem" (Akerlof, 1970) where low-quality journals cannot be distinguished from high-quality ones, so average quality declines. This warrants consumer protection, not evolutionary analysis.

**Response**: The market failure model cannot explain three key findings:

First, **evolutionary persistence**: Market inefficiencies typically correct over time as reputation systems develop and information spreads. Predatory publishing has intensified over 15+ years despite widespread awareness. This persistence indicates stable equilibrium, not transient inefficiency.

Second, **mimetic sophistication**: Parasitic publishers invest in legitimacy signals (ISSN registration, editorial boards, indexing claims) specifically to game reputation systems. This is active deception, not passive information asymmetry.

Third, **coordination failure**: Many researchers knowingly submit to suspect venues because unilateral avoidance is costly (foregone publication speed) while collective avoidance is not coordinated. This is a strategic interaction problem (game theory), not merely an information problem (market failure).

PSO synthesizes insights from market failure (information asymmetry matters) with evolutionary dynamics (patterns adapt to interventions) and game theory (coordination failures maintain equilibria). It is a richer explanation than market inefficiency alone.

### Objection 3: "Rational Exchange, Not Parasitism"

**Claim**: Predatory journals provide services researchers value (rapid publication, guaranteed acceptance, CV lines). Researchers knowingly pay for these services. Both parties benefit from voluntary exchange. There is no parasitism, just a market for fast credentialing.

**Response**: This objection confuses individual rationality with social welfare:

First, **negative externalities**: When predatory submissions proliferate, they corrupt bibliographic databases, degrade citation networks, and erode public trust in science. These costs are not internalized by the parties to the transaction. Just as pollution can be individually rational but socially harmful, predatory publishing can be individually rational but socially parasitic.

Second, **metric distortion**: Researchers "benefit" from predatory publication only because institutional evaluation systems reward publication counts. This is a distorted benefit created by upstream institutional failures. Treating this as genuine value creation is like treating addiction as beneficial because addicts value drugs. The demand itself is pathological.

Third, **asymmetric value extraction**: Researchers pay fees and sacrifice reputation (even if reputational cost is low, it is nonzero). Publishers provide minimal service (no substantive peer review, negligible editorial investment). The exchange involves real resource transfer (money from authors to publishers) without commensurate service provision. This fits the definition of parasitism: asymmetric extraction without reciprocal benefit.

The rational choice framework correctly identifies that researchers respond optimally to incentives but fails to explain why those incentives are distorted. PSO addresses this gap by analyzing how metric-based evaluation systems create selection pressures that favor parasitic equilibria.

### Objection 4: "Cultural Differences, Not Parasitism"

**Claim**: What Western academics perceive as "predatory" may reflect different cultural norms about academic publishing. Journals in developing countries may have different editorial practices (rapid review, author solicitation, flexible scope) without being parasitic. The PSO framework imposes Western standards universally.

**Response**: The cultural relativism objection fails for four reasons:

First, **pattern independence from geography**: Parasitic publishers operate globally (US, UK, India, Egypt, Turkey, China). The patterns documented here are not specific to any region. The legitimate control case (Bon View Publishing, Hong Kong-based) demonstrates that non-Western publishers can maintain rigorous standards.

Second, **deception, not difference**: PSO is defined by **mimicry of legitimacy combined with value extraction**, not merely by different practices. A journal that transparently offers rapid publication for a fee without claiming rigorous peer review is not parasitic (it is honest about the exchange). Parasitic journals **falsely claim** peer review rigor, disciplinary fit, and editorial selectivity while providing none of these.

Third, **cross-cultural harm**: Researchers in developing countries are disproportionately victimized by predatory publishers (Cobey et al., 2018; Shen & Björk, 2015), undermining the claim that these practices reflect local norms. If predatory publishing were culturally appropriate in certain contexts, local researchers would not report being deceived.

Fourth, **theoretical generality**: PSO is defined by evolutionary dynamics (replication through extended phenotypes, selection for extraction-maximizing traits, mimicry to evade detection), not by cultural standards. These dynamics operate universally wherever institutional selection occurs.

The cultural differences objection conflates legitimate diversity in editorial practices with systematic deceptive value extraction. PSO theory respects the former while identifying the latter.

### Objection 5: "Nascent Journals Lack Resources, Not Intent"

**Claim**: New journals may exhibit some "predatory" characteristics (rapid review, author solicitation, economic opacity) not from parasitic intent but from resource constraints. They lack established editorial boards, efficient review systems, and financial stability. As they mature, they improve. Condemning all new journals as parasitic unfairly prevents legitimate competition.

**Response**: This objection raises a valid concern but conflates two distinct phenomena:

First, **legitimate nascent journals exist** and can be distinguished from parasitic ones through key indicators:

- **Disciplinary coherence**: Legitimate new journals focus on specific scholarly areas, attracting submissions within their scope. Parasitic journals solicit indiscriminately.
- **Editorial investment**: Legitimate new journals recruit qualified editors, develop review infrastructure, and seek indexing. These investments may be incomplete initially but trajectory is toward increasing rigor. Parasitic journals make no such investments.
- **Transparency**: Legitimate new journals openly acknowledge being new, disclose current limitations (e.g., "not yet indexed in Scopus but seeking inclusion"), and provide realistic timelines. Parasitic journals make false claims about established status.
- **Governance**: Legitimate new journals have identifiable leadership with verifiable institutional affiliations and scholarly records. Parasitic journals use fabricated or anonymous personnel.

Second, **the PSO Index accommodates uncertainty**: A nascent legitimate journal might score 0.30-0.40 (unclear range) on the PSO Index due to resource constraints. The model does not falsely classify such journals as definitively parasitic (threshold 0.60). Boundary cases warrant cautious evaluation, not automatic rejection. But journals scoring 0.80+ exhibit systematic characteristics (disciplinary indifference, emotional manipulation, economic opacity, automation) that cannot be explained by resource constraints alone.

Third, **temporal trajectories differ**: Legitimate nascent journals improve over time (lower PSO scores as infrastructure develops). Parasitic journals remain stable or intensify parasitic characteristics (higher mimetic legitimacy without substantive improvement). Longitudinal observation distinguishes the two.

The key distinction is **directional intent and investment**. Legitimate nascent journals aim toward rigorous standards and invest in reaching them, even if current capacity is limited. Parasitic journals mimic standards superficially while minimizing actual investment.

### Objection 6: "No Intentional Conspiracy Required"

**Claim**: PSO theory emphasizes spontaneous coordination without conspiracy. But if there is no intentional coordination, how can the pattern be "parasitic"? Parasitism implies intent to harm. Without intentional deception, the pattern is merely emergent market behavior, not parasitic.

**Response**: This objection misunderstands the concept of institutional parasitism:

First, **biological parasitism lacks intent**. Parasitic organisms (tapeworms, ticks, cuckoos) do not "intend" to harm hosts; they evolved to extract resources because such strategies enhance reproductive fitness. Harm to hosts is byproduct, not purpose. Similarly, institutional parasitism does not require malicious intent by publishers. The parasitic character lies in the **structure of the relationship** (asymmetric extraction, mimicry, value destruction), not in subjective mental states.

Second, **spontaneous order can be pathological**. Hayek himself recognized that spontaneous coordination can produce harmful equilibria. The term "parasitic" describes the systematic effect (value extraction without reciprocity), not the motivation. Just as a "vicious cycle" does not imply viciousness in actors, "parasitic order" does not imply parasitic intent.

Third, **extended phenotype logic**: Parasitic characteristics emerge because they enhance institutional replication, not because individuals consciously choose to harm the system. Publishers who accidentally discover that disciplinary indifference expands their target pool gain competitive advantage. Over time, this trait becomes prevalent because it is selected for. No conspiracy is needed; evolutionary dynamics suffice.

Fourth, **policy implications remain valid**: Whether parasitic publishers consciously intend harm or stumble into effective extraction strategies is irrelevant for policy. The structural relationships remain parasitic (asymmetric extraction, mimicry, value destruction), and interventions must target those structures regardless of intent.

The objection reveals a common confusion between **individual motivation and systemic function**. PSO is a systemic-level concept; it describes institutional patterns, not individual psychology.

### Objection 7: "Why No Self-Correction?"

**Claim**: If predatory publishing harms researchers and degrades science, competitive market pressure should eliminate it. Researchers burned by predatory journals will warn others; universities that hire faculty with predatory publications will adjust tenure criteria; funding agencies will exclude predatory venues from CVs. Why has this self-correction not occurred?

**Response**: Several mechanisms prevent self-correction:

First, **diffuse costs, concentrated benefits**: Individual researchers bear small costs (APC fee, minor reputational damage) while publishers capture concentrated benefits (revenue). This asymmetry prevents effective collective action. Compare to environmental pollution: individual consumers bear diffuse pollution costs while firms capture concentrated profits, preventing market self-correction without regulation.

Second, **coordination failures**: Self-correction requires coordinated action (all researchers boycott predatory venues; all universities reject predatory publications from tenure files). But unilateral action is costly. The first university to exclude predatory publications from tenure review may lose competitive advantage in faculty recruitment. Coordination requires institutional mechanisms (consortia, regulatory mandates) that currently do not exist.

Third, **information persistence problems**: Warning systems (Beall's List) face legal threats and sustainability challenges. Social media warnings are ephemeral and unsystematic. No durable, authoritative registry of predatory publishers exists with global coverage. Information decay prevents accumulation of collective knowledge.

Fourth, **evolutionary adaptation**: As detection mechanisms improve, parasitic forms adapt. Early predatory journals used obvious signals (Gmail addresses, poor grammar, nonexistent editorial boards). Modern parasitic publishers invest in mimicry (professional domains, ISSN registration, fabricated editorial boards), defeating simplistic detection rules. This is an evolutionary arms race; parasites adapt faster than detection systems.

Fifth, **metric capture**: As long as institutional evaluation systems (tenure review, visa applications, grant scoring) reward publication counts, demand for rapid publication persists. Market pressure alone cannot eliminate parasitic supply when institutional demand remains robust.

Self-correction requires coordinated institutional intervention to change the selection pressures. Markets correct inefficiencies when feedback mechanisms function properly. But when selection pressures reward parasitic strategies (publish-or-perish metrics) and penalties for parasitism are weak (diffuse reputational costs), market dynamics sustain rather than eliminate parasitism.

### Objection 8: "PSO Is Unfalsifiable"

**Claim**: PSO theory can explain any institutional pattern post-hoc. If a journal exhibits disciplinary mismatch, that is evidence of parasitism. If it exhibits disciplinary match, that could be sophisticated mimicry. If it charges high APCs, that is value extraction. If it charges low APCs or offers waivers, that is a strategy to attract submissions. The theory is too flexible to be scientifically falsifiable.

**Response**: This objection mischaracterizes the theory's structure:

First, **ex-ante predictions**: PSO theory generated specific, testable predictions before observing the full corpus:

- **Prediction 1**: Parasitic invitations will exhibit disciplinary mismatch because automation maximizes target pool. **Result**: 85.7% confirmed (6/7 severe mismatch).
- **Prediction 2**: Parasitic invitations will deploy emotional manipulation tactics targeting known cognitive biases. **Result**: 100% confirmed (all parasitic cases use multiple tactics).
- **Prediction 3**: Parasitic invitations will reduce submission friction to maximize response rates. **Result**: 100% confirmed (all parasitic cases bypass editorial management systems).
- **Prediction 4**: Parasitic invitations will exhibit automation indicators because scalability requires low marginal cost. **Result**: 85.7% confirmed (template language, batch timing).

These predictions were derived from theoretical principles (evolutionary selection for replication-enhancing traits), not inductively discovered from data. They could have been disconfirmed.

Second, **falsification conditions**: PSO theory would be falsified if:

- Parasitic invitations exhibited strong disciplinary match (disconfirmed: 0/7 cases)
- Parasitic invitations used emotionally neutral language (disconfirmed: 0/7 cases)
- Parasitic invitations required standard editorial management system submission (disconfirmed: 0/7 cases)
- Parasitic invitations showed evidence of individualized editorial review (disconfirmed: 0/7 cases)

Third, **discriminant validity**: PSO theory makes distinct predictions from alternative frameworks. The fraud model predicts predatory publishers hide identities and use obfuscation; PSO predicts mimicry of legitimacy (correct). Market failure model predicts certification systems solve the problem; PSO predicts adaptation through mimicry (correct). Rational choice model predicts education reduces victimization; PSO predicts persistence despite awareness (correct).

Fourth, **quantitative thresholds**: The PSO Index provides precise classification thresholds (0.20 = legitimate; 0.60 = highly parasitic) set a priori. If empirical cases had been evenly distributed across the 0-1 range with no clustering, the theory would be disconfirmed. Instead, clear bimodal distribution obtained (legitimate case 0.08; parasitic cases 0.78-0.94).

PSO theory is falsifiable in principle and has survived empirical tests. The objection confuses theoretical flexibility (capacity to explain multiple pathways to parasitism) with unfalsifiability (inability to make disconfirmable predictions).

### Objection 9: "Individual Publishers May Be Honest"

**Claim**: Individual editorial board members, managing editors, and publishers associated with "predatory" journals may genuinely believe they are providing legitimate scholarly service. They may not understand peer review standards, may come from different academic cultures, or may be honest but incompetent. Labeling them "parasitic" is unfair if they are sincerely trying to contribute.

**Response**: This objection confuses **individual sincerity with institutional function**:

First, **PSO is a system-level concept**: The parasitic character lies in the institutional pattern (asymmetric extraction, mimicry, value destruction), not in individual intentions. Just as an economic recession harms people regardless of whether any individual "intended" the recession, parasitic institutional forms extract value regardless of whether participants are consciously deceptive.

Second, **analogy to biological parasitism**: Parasitic organisms do not "intend" harm; they evolved extraction strategies. A tapeworm is not morally culpable, but the relationship remains parasitic (asymmetric extraction, host harm). Similarly, a publisher may sincerely believe their rapid-review model serves scholarship while the structural relationship remains parasitic (authors pay fees, receive minimal genuine service, scientific literature is corrupted).

Third, **extended phenotype framework**: PSO theory analyzes downstream effects, not mental states. Whether a publisher is honest-but-incompetent or deliberately deceptive, the institutional form produces the same extended phenotype (invitations with disciplinary mismatch, emotional manipulation, friction reduction). Selection operates on effects, not intentions.

Fourth, **honest incompetence is still harmful**: If a publisher genuinely does not understand peer review standards, publishes without substantive editorial oversight, and charges fees for this non-service, the outcome is still value extraction without reciprocal benefit. Harm persists regardless of subjective innocence.

Fifth, **policy implications**: Effective interventions target structural vulnerabilities, not individual morality. Whether publishers are villains or innocents, the solution is the same: reform metric systems that create demand, increase transparency to reduce information asymmetry, coordinate collective rejection. The theory guides policy without requiring moral judgments about individuals.

This objection reflects discomfort with structural analysis that abstracts from individual agency. But social science regularly analyzes systemic patterns (recessions, poverty traps, arms races) without assigning individual blame. PSO theory operates at the same level of abstraction.

### Objection 10: "Framework Overgeneralizes"

**Claim**: Applying PSO logic to diverse domains (academic publishing, finance, regulation, credentialing) risks vacuous overgeneralization. If any asymmetric exchange can be labeled "parasitic spontaneous order," the concept loses analytical precision. Parsimony demands narrower domain-specific explanations rather than grand unified theories.

**Response**: The generality objection misunderstands scientific theorizing:

First, **unification is a theoretical virtue**: Physics does not develop separate theories of gravity for planets, projectiles, and tides; it develops universal gravitation. Economics does not develop separate theories of supply and demand for wheat, steel, and labor; it develops general equilibrium theory. Theoretical unification is desirable when diverse phenomena share underlying causal structure. PSO unifies predatory journals, payday lending, credential inflation, and regulatory capture by identifying shared dynamics (emergent coordination, mimicry, asymmetric extraction, evolutionary stability).

Second, **domain-specific predictions**: PSO makes different predictions in different domains based on domain-specific selection pressures. In academic publishing, PSO predicts disciplinary indifference (because automation maximizes reach); in financial predation, PSO predicts complexity (because opacity facilitates extraction); in credentialing, PSO predicts inflation (because unilateral deviation is penalized). The general framework generates domain-specific hypotheses, not vacuous generalities.

Third, **parsimony vs. realism trade-off**: Narrower domain-specific theories may fit specific cases more precisely but sacrifice cross-domain learning and predictive power in novel contexts. PSO theory sacrifices some domain-specific fit for cross-domain generality. This is a standard trade-off in science. Whether the trade-off is worthwhile depends on empirical performance. PSO has successfully predicted patterns in multiple domains; domain-specific alternatives have not generated comparable cross-domain insights.

Fourth, **falsification through failed extension**: If PSO framework failed to explain new domains, that would count against it. The successful extension to citation rings, regulatory capture, and credential inflation (documented in supplementary materials) supports rather than undermines the theory. Failed extensions would falsify overgeneralization.

Fifth, **analytical precision preserved through formal definition**: PSO has six defining characteristics (see Theoretical Framework section). Not all asymmetric exchanges are PSO; only those meeting all six criteria. This preserves analytical precision while enabling generalization.

The overgeneralization objection is premature. The theory should be judged by empirical performance across domains, not rejected a priori for theoretical ambition.

## Cross-Domain Extensions: Evidence from Related Contexts

The PSO framework predicts similar patterns should emerge wherever analogous selection pressures exist. This section documents extensions to seven related domains, providing external validation.

### Domain 1: Conference Invitations

Academic conferences increasingly solicit papers through automated invitations resembling predatory journal patterns. The author received four conference invitations during the observation period exhibiting:

- **Disciplinary mismatch**: Legal theory paper invited to conferences on engineering, medicine, tourism, and nanotechnology.
- **Mimetic legitimacy**: Professional formatting, sponsored by obscure "international associations."
- **Emotional manipulation**: "Honored to invite you as keynote speaker" (unpaid), "Limited slots available."
- **Economic opacity**: Registration fees disclosed only after abstract acceptance.
- **Automation indicators**: Template language, simultaneous batch invitations, exact title scraping.

The parallel suggests **conference-based PSO** operates through identical mechanisms: automated database scraping, emotional manipulation to secure registrations, mimicry of legitimate academic conferences, and revenue extraction through registration fees.

### Domain 2: Editorial Board Recruitment

Predatory journals recruit editorial board members to enhance legitimacy. Board membership requires no actual service (no manuscript handling, no policy decisions) but provides CV lines and "Associate Editor" titles. This is a **credential PSO**: the institutional form extracts legitimacy from scholars' affiliations while providing zero genuine editorial responsibility.

The Diago case (supplementary materials) confirms this pattern. A fabricated persona with plagiarized publications received multiple editorial board invitations from journals that never verified identity, credentials, or expertise.

### Domain 3: Peer Review Platforms

Commercial peer review services (e.g., Publons, now Web of Science Reviewer Recognition) gamify peer review through badges, points, and leaderboards. While legitimate in origin, the system creates incentives for "review mills" where individuals offer rapid superficial reviews to accumulate credits. This is a **gamification PSO**: metrics intended to recognize quality service instead incentivize quantity gaming.

### Domain 4: Citation Manipulation Services

Commercial services sell "citation packages" where authors pay to have their work cited by papers in affiliated journals. This creates mutual citation rings that inflate bibliometric indicators without intellectual contribution (Fister et al., 2016). The pattern exhibits PSO characteristics:

- **Spontaneous coordination**: Individual authors independently purchase services; no conspiracy required.
- **Mimicry**: Citations appear identical to legitimate scholarly references.
- **Asymmetric extraction**: Authors pay fees; citation providers produce formulaic papers with minimal intellectual content.
- **Evolutionary stability**: As detection improves (algorithms identify suspicious citation clusters), services adapt (distribute citations across more journals, vary timing, use realistic-sounding paper titles).

### Domain 5: Open Access Fee Waivers

Some parasitic journals advertise "APC waivers for all authors" to attract submissions, then inform authors after acceptance that "unfortunately, funding constraints require payment." This **bait-and-switch PSO** exploits sunk cost bias: authors who invested effort in submission and revision are more likely to pay fees than if costs were disclosed upfront.

### Domain 6: Book Chapter Invitations

Predatory publishers solicit book chapter contributions, compile them without substantive editing, and sell the resulting volumes at exorbitant prices to libraries. Authors gain CV lines ("book chapter in international edited volume"), publishers gain sales revenue, and libraries acquire low-quality content. This is a **three-party PSO**: value flows from libraries to publishers, authors receive credentials, and genuine scholarly communication is displaced.

### Domain 7: Mega-Journal Proliferation

Legitimate publishers (Springer Nature, Elsevier, Wiley) have launched "mega-journals" (e.g., Scientific Reports, Heliyon) with minimal peer review, broad scope, and high acceptance rates (60-90%). These journals are not fraudulent but exhibit structural similarities to parasitic publishing:

- **Broad scope** (interdisciplinary acceptance)
- **High acceptance rates** (revenue through volume)
- **Minimal editorial filtering** (soundness-only review)
- **APC revenue model** (profit maximization through throughput)

The key distinction is **transparency**: mega-journals openly describe their editorial model, provide rigorous technical review (if not novelty evaluation), and are indexed in legitimate databases. But the structural convergence suggests **parasitic forms exert selection pressure on legitimate publishers**. Market competition pushes legitimate publishers toward parasitic characteristics (high acceptance, rapid publication, volume-based revenue), creating an evolutionary race to the bottom.

## Policy Implications and Structural Interventions

### Why Current Interventions Fail

Standard policy responses to predatory publishing have proven ineffective:

**1. Blacklisting (Beall's List, Predatory Reports)**: Journals simply rename or launch new brands. The institutional form persists through different corporate vehicles.

**2. Education (Think.Check.Submit campaigns)**: Awareness increases but behavior does not change. Researchers facing publish-or-perish pressure knowingly submit to suspect venues because institutional incentives reward publication counts.

**3. Whitelisting (Directory of Open Access Journals)**: Predatory publishers game certification criteria by adopting surface features (ISSN registration, editorial board lists, peer review claims) without substantive changes.

**4. Voluntary Publisher Codes (Committee on Publication Ethics)**: Voluntary codes lack enforcement. Publishers who adopt parasitic strategies gain competitive advantage over ethical competitors, creating adverse selection.

**5. Researcher Pledges (anti-predatory submission pledges)**: Collective action fails due to free-rider problems. Individual researchers who forgo predatory venues sacrifice publication speed without systemic benefit.

These interventions target symptoms (individual publishers, individual researchers) rather than the structural conditions that maintain parasitic equilibria: metric distortion, information asymmetry, coordination failure, and selection pressures.

### Structural Interventions: Altering Selection Pressures

Effective policy must change the institutional environment that makes parasitic strategies viable. Four categories of intervention target different structural vulnerabilities:

#### Intervention Category 1: Metric Reform

**Problem**: Publication-count metrics create demand for rapid publication regardless of quality.

**Solutions**:

1. **Quality-weighted assessment**: Replace publication counts with quality-weighted metrics. Tenure and promotion decisions should evaluate the impact, rigor, and contribution of work, not merely quantity. This is labor-intensive but already practiced in elite institutions.

2. **Narrative evaluation**: Require researchers to submit narrative descriptions of their most important contributions rather than comprehensive CV publication lists. This shifts focus from counting to substantive evaluation.

3. **Field-normalized benchmarks**: Use disciplinary standards rather than universal metrics. In mathematics, 10 publications may indicate strong productivity; in medicine, 50 publications may be modest. Counting without normalization distorts incentives.

4. **Eliminate count-based thresholds**: Remove explicit publication count requirements from tenure rubrics, grant applications, and visa criteria. Replace with qualitative descriptions of required scholarly impact.

**Expected effect**: Reducing demand for quantity would eliminate the market niche that parasitic publishers exploit. If publication speed no longer conveys career advantage, researchers would optimize for prestige and impact instead.

**Challenges**: Metric reform requires coordinated action across universities, funding agencies, and governments. Unilateral reform creates competitive disadvantage (universities with quality standards may lose recruitment battles to universities with count standards). International coordination is particularly difficult.

#### Intervention Category 2: Transparency Mandates

**Problem**: Information asymmetry allows parasitic publishers to mimic legitimacy.

**Solutions**:

1. **Mandatory disclosure before submission**: Require journals to disclose acceptance rates, median review timelines, APC structure, editorial board qualifications, and indexing status on submission portals before authors commit effort. Standardize disclosure format to enable comparison.

2. **Public registry of editorial practices**: Create a global database where journals report standardized information (acceptance rates, review processes, financial structure). Make this information searchable and comparable. Fund the registry through stakeholders (universities, funding agencies) to ensure independence from publishers.

3. **Verified editor credentials**: Require editorial board members to confirm participation through institutional email verification. Public database flags journals with unverified or fabricated editorial boards.

4. **Transparent post-publication metrics**: Require journals to report post-publication correction rates, retraction rates, citation patterns, and authorship diversity. High correction rates or suspicious citation patterns signal quality problems.

**Expected effect**: Transparency reduces mimicry effectiveness. Parasitic publishers cannot falsely claim rigorous review if acceptance rates (e.g., 95%) are publicly disclosed. Information disclosure shifts competitive advantage toward genuinely selective journals.

**Challenges**: Enforcement requires regulatory authority. Voluntary disclosure fails (parasitic publishers simply do not participate). International cooperation needed to prevent jurisdictional arbitrage.

#### Intervention Category 3: Collective Action Coordination

**Problem**: Coordination failure prevents effective boycotts and collective responses.

**Solutions**:

1. **Disciplinary consortia**: Form consortia of scholarly societies in each discipline to certify legitimate venues and coordinate rejection of parasitic submissions. Consortia provide coordination mechanism that individual researchers lack. Example: "Association of Law Schools Certified Journals" list.

2. **University coalition agreements**: Groups of universities collectively adopt uniform tenure standards excluding predatory publications. Coordination eliminates competitive disadvantage of unilateral reform.

3. **Funding agency coordination**: Major funding agencies (NIH, NSF, ERC, Wellcome Trust) collectively adopt policies excluding predatory publications from grant applications and progress reports. Coordinated enforcement changes researcher incentives.

4. **Visa authority cooperation**: Immigration authorities in major destination countries (US, UK, Canada, Australia) collectively exclude predatory publications from academic visa applications. This eliminates a major demand source (developing-country researchers seeking academic visas).

**Expected effect**: Coordinated collective action solves the free-rider problem. If all universities simultaneously exclude predatory publications from tenure review, no individual university suffers competitive disadvantage. If all funding agencies simultaneously exclude predatory publications from CVs, no individual researcher gains advantage from parasitic submission.

**Challenges**: Coordination requires institutional trust, shared standards, and enforcement mechanisms. Different disciplines may resist uniform criteria. Political barriers to international cooperation on immigration policy.

#### Intervention Category 4: Structural Buffers

**Problem**: Direct links between publication counts and high-stakes outcomes (tenure, visas, grants) create intense pressure to game metrics.

**Solutions**:

1. **Career stage flexibility**: Extend tenure clocks, provide post-tenure sabbaticals, and reduce publication pressure for early-career scholars. This reduces time-pressure incentive to seek rapid publication.

2. **Alternative career pathways**: Create non-research academic tracks (teaching-focused positions, clinical positions, science communication roles) with different evaluation criteria. This reduces pool of researchers desperate for publication counts.

3. **Grant decoupling**: Fund researchers based on proposed projects and track records rather than recent publication velocity. Use narrative evaluation of past contributions rather than publication counts in grant scoring.

4. **Visa pathway diversity**: Create academic visa categories based on institutional sponsorship rather than individual publication metrics. University affirms candidate's qualifications; immigration authority does not independently count publications.

**Expected effect**: Reducing stakes of publication counts reduces desperation that drives parasitic submission. Researchers with secure positions, diverse career options, and evaluation based on quality have no incentive to game metrics.

**Challenges**: Structural buffers reduce accountability. Extending tenure clocks may protect unproductive researchers, not just those facing predatory incentives. Alternative career tracks may become dumping grounds for failed researchers rather than valued pathways. The challenge is designing buffers that reduce metric gaming without eliminating quality standards.

### Synthesis: Changing the Evolutionary Environment

Effective intervention requires simultaneous action across multiple levels:

- **Demand side** (metric reform, structural buffers): Reduce researcher incentives to submit to parasitic venues
- **Supply side** (transparency mandates): Increase costs of mimicry for parasitic publishers
- **Coordination** (collective action): Solve free-rider problems that prevent voluntary boycotts

No single intervention suffices. Blacklisting fails because demand persists. Education fails because incentives dominate knowledge. Voluntary pledges fail because coordination cannot be sustained.

The evolutionary logic of PSO reveals why: the institutional form is selected for by the environment (metric-based evaluation, information asymmetry, coordination failure). Eliminating the form requires changing the environment. This is politically difficult but theoretically necessary.

The analogy to public health is instructive: eliminating infectious disease requires not just treating sick individuals (analogous to shutting down individual predatory journals) but changing environmental conditions (sanitation, vaccination, vector control) that allow disease to spread. Similarly, eliminating parasitic publishing requires not just targeting individual publishers but changing institutional conditions (metric systems, transparency, coordination) that allow parasitic forms to thrive.

## Limitations and Future Research Directions

This study faces several limitations that future research should address:

### Empirical Limitations

1. **Sample size**: Eight invitations from one researcher limit generalizability. Future research should compile larger corpora across multiple researchers, fields, and countries.

2. **Observational design**: Natural experiment provides strong internal validity but limits causal inference. Future research could conduct field experiments (e.g., creating fabricated scholar profiles with varying characteristics to test which traits attract parasitic invitations).

3. **Single source paper**: All invitations referenced the same published work. Future research should analyze invitation patterns across researchers with diverse publication profiles.

4. **Temporal snapshot**: 18-month observation captures current tactics but not long-term evolutionary dynamics. Longitudinal studies tracking changes in parasitic strategies over decades would illuminate adaptation patterns.

5. **Geographic concentration**: Author based in Argentina with Spanish-language institutional affiliation may attract different parasitic publishers than researchers in US, Europe, or Asia. Cross-national comparative studies needed.

### Theoretical Limitations

1. **Boundary conditions**: The study identifies PSO in academic publishing but does not fully specify boundary conditions (under what conditions does PSO emerge vs. legitimate spontaneous order?). Formal modeling could derive necessary and sufficient conditions.

2. **Equilibrium multiplicity**: The game-theoretic analysis suggests multiple equilibria (legitimate vs. parasitic) but does not predict which equilibrium obtains in which contexts. Comparative institutional analysis across countries with different equilibria would illuminate selection factors.

3. **Evolutionary dynamics**: The study documents a snapshot of parasitic characteristics but does not trace evolutionary adaptation over time. Historical analysis of how predatory publishing evolved from early forms (simple email spam) to sophisticated forms (ISSN registration, editorial board mimicry) would test evolutionary predictions.

4. **Mechanism precision**: The study identifies cognitive biases and structural vulnerabilities that parasitic publishers exploit but does not experimentally isolate causal effects of each mechanism. Controlled experiments manipulating invitation characteristics could identify which features most strongly drive submission behavior.

### Policy Limitations

1. **Intervention testing**: The study proposes structural interventions but does not empirically test their effectiveness. Natural experiments (e.g., countries that reformed tenure metrics vs. those that did not) or controlled trials (e.g., universities randomly assigned to different evaluation systems) could provide causal evidence.

2. **Implementation feasibility**: Proposed interventions (metric reform, transparency mandates, coordinated collective action) face political and logistical barriers not analyzed in this study. Implementation science research could identify practical pathways to policy adoption.

3. **Unintended consequences**: Structural interventions may generate unanticipated effects (e.g., metric reform may advantage researchers skilled at narrative self-promotion over those producing rigorous but underpromoted work). Pilot implementations with careful monitoring needed before large-scale rollout.

### Future Research Agenda

Building on this study, future research should:

1. **Expand empirical scope**: Compile large-scale corpora of predatory invitations across fields, countries, and career stages. Use machine learning to automate PSO Index scoring and identify patterns invisible to qualitative analysis.

2. **Conduct field experiments**: Create fabricated scholar profiles varying in characteristics (field, career stage, publication venue, institutional affiliation) to test which factors attract predatory invitations. This would causally identify targeting strategies.

3. **Trace evolutionary history**: Document how predatory publishing tactics evolved over 20+ years through archival analysis of email corpora, web scraping of journal websites over time, and interviews with publishers.

4. **Formal modeling**: Develop computational agent-based models of academic publishing ecosystems to simulate evolutionary dynamics under different institutional conditions. Test which interventions successfully shift equilibria from parasitic to legitimate.

5. **Cross-domain comparison**: Apply PSO framework to other domains (payday lending, credential inflation, citation manipulation) to test generalizability and identify domain-specific vs. universal features.

6. **Intervention trials**: Partner with universities, funding agencies, or countries willing to pilot structural interventions. Use randomized controlled trials or quasi-experimental designs to measure causal effects on predatory publishing prevalence.

7. **Comparative institutional analysis**: Compare countries with high vs. low predatory publishing rates, identifying institutional differences (metric systems, tenure structures, regulatory approaches) that predict outcomes.

8. **Cognitive experiments**: Conduct experiments with researcher populations to identify which invitation characteristics most effectively exploit cognitive biases and which interventions (e.g., warning labels, decision aids) reduce vulnerability.

This research agenda would transform predatory publishing from a practical nuisance into a model system for understanding institutional evolution, parasitic spontaneous order, and the evolutionary dynamics of regulatory interventions. The insights gained would generalize to other domains exhibiting similar pathologies.
