# Case Study: The Diago Experiment

**Researcher:** Pascual D. Diago (University of Valencia)  
**Experiment:** Submission of deliberately absurd paper to test predatory journal review  
**Source:** Documented by Pablo Malo and shared in Spanish academic circles  
**Date:** ~2023-2024  
**Significance:** External validation of PSO framework predictions

---

## The Experiment

### Fabricated Paper Characteristics

**Title:** [Absurd combination of obstetrics and Fibonacci sequences]

**Content:**
- Nonsensical mix of medical terminology and mathematical concepts
- Fake references (authors and journals invented)
- Meaningless graphs and figures
- Obvious logical contradictions
- No coherent methodology

**Purpose:** Test whether predatory journals actually conduct peer review

---

## Results

### Acceptance Details

**Timeline:**
- Submission → Acceptance: **Minutes to hours** (not weeks/months)
- Multiple journals accepted without revision
- Some requested minor "formatting" changes (not content)

**Process "claimed":**
- "Double-blind peer review"
- "Rigorous editorial standards"
- "Expert evaluation"

**Process actual:**
- Automated acceptance system
- No human reviewer engagement
- Content completely ignored

### Publication

**Outcome:**
- Paper published with DOI
- Indexed in OpenAlex
- Citeable in academic databases
- Remains online (in some venues)

**Cost:**
- APC charged (amounts varied by journal)
- Payment confirmed publication (no review gatekeeping)

---

## Significance for PSO Framework

### 1. Functional Divergence Validated

**Claimed:** Peer review validates quality  
**Reality:** No review occurs, only payment verification

**Prediction from EPT:**
- Phenotype (journal structure) mimics function
- But actual function diverges (credential vending, not validation)

**Diago result:** Confirms complete divergence

### 2. Content Blindness Demonstrated

**System is blind to:**
- Semantic coherence
- Methodological validity
- Logical consistency
- Reference authenticity

**System is sensitive to:**
- Payment readiness
- Formatting compliance (superficial)
- Submission format (structural)

**Interpretation:** Selection operates on form, not content

### 3. Mimicry Effectiveness Proven

**Despite absurd content:**
- Paper received DOI (looks legitimate)
- Indexed in databases (appears in searches)
- Publisher used academic language
- Process mimicked peer review

**Result:** Mimicry successful at institutional interface layer

---

## Extended Phenotype Analysis

### Replicator: "Published Paper" Token

**Diago's experiment proves:**

The token value is **independent of content quality**

**Evidence:**
1. Absurd paper published
2. Same treatment as legitimate submissions
3. No quality discrimination
4. Payment sufficient for "publication"

**Implication:** Selection pressure acts on symbolic form, not epistemic substance

### Niche Stability

**Why PSO persists despite Diago-type exposés:**

1. **Information asymmetry:** Most authors/institutions don't know about these experiments
2. **Verification cost:** Checking venue quality is expensive
3. **Credential value:** "Published with DOI" still has residual value
4. **Mimicry effectiveness:** Signals (ISSN, DOI, "peer review") are cheap to copy

**Result:** Niche remains stable even after exposure

**Cuckoo eggs in host nest:**

- Host bird doesn't detect difference
- Host raises cuckoo chick (divergent function)

## Implications for Detection

### What Diago Proves About Filtering

**Content-based filtering insufficient:**
- Absurd content passed through
- Semantic analysis would flag Diago's paper
- But PSO systems don't use semantic analysis

**Structural signals insufficient:**
- DOI, indexing, ISSN all present
- Paper "looks" legitimate in databases
- Automated systems can't distinguish

**Only reliable filter:**
- **Venue verification** (check journal in DOAJ, Scopus)
- **Human judgment** on publisher credibility
- **Institutional whitelist/blacklist** maintenance

- Tests "peer review" claim empirically

**Result:** **Falsification of peer review claim** for predatory journals

**Comparison to this corpus:**
- This corpus: Observational (real invitations received)
- Diago: Experimental (deliberate intervention)

## Ethical Considerations

### Was Diago's Experiment Justified?

**Arguments for:**
- Exposes harmful practices
- Protects future researchers
- Validates theoretical predictions
- Minimal harm (no real patient data, etc.)

**Arguments against:**
- Contributes to "noise" in academic record
- Paper remains indexed (pollutes databases)
- Could be misused if someone cites it unknowingly

**Consensus (in Spanish academic community):** 
**Justified as whistleblowing** against predatory practices

## Reproducibility

### Others Can (and Should) Replicate

**Method:**
1. Create obviously flawed paper
2. Submit to suspected predatory venues
3. Document acceptance/rejection
4. Publish results as methodological note

**Benefits:**
- Crowdsourced verification
- Identifies specific predatory journals
- Educates community
- Pressures venues to improve (or exposes them)

**Note:** Must be done carefully to avoid legal issues (libel, defamation)

## Integration with PSO Framework

### Diago as Stress Test

**Question PSO framework must address:**
"Why would a system accept demonstrably absurd content?"

**EPT answer:**
- System optimizes for payment extraction, not quality validation
- Content evaluation costs resources (reviewer time)
- Absurd content doesn't reduce payment probability
- **Therefore: No selection pressure for content quality**

**Diago validates this prediction empirically**
